This skill guides the creation, revision, and professional formatting of resumes (CVs), cover letters, and selection criteria addendums. It works in two modes:

1. **Targeted** — The user provides a specific job description. Tailor all documents to match that role's requirements, keywords, and selection criteria.
2. **General** — The user targets a role category (e.g., "engineering manager," "data analyst," "chef"). Build documents around the core competencies and expectations for that category.

**Adjacent skill:** This skill integrates with **`professional-development-fyi`** to help the user identify and articulate their marketable strengths using the Lominger/FYI 67-competency framework. When helping a user identify what to highlight, consult that skill for competency-based coaching.

## Part I: The Resume Workflow

### Phase 1: Discovery

Gather information from the user before writing anything:

1. **The target role.** Job description (if available), role title, industry, level.
2. **Their background.** Current role, career history, education, certifications, key accomplishments.
3. **Their strengths.** What are they known for? What do they do better than most? (Use `professional-development-fyi` competencies to structure this conversation.)
4. **Their constraints.** Career gaps? Career changes? Over/under-qualified? Relocating?
5. **Document requirements.** Resume only? Cover letter? Selection criteria? What format and length?

### Phase 2: Strategy

Before writing, determine the strategy:

**Keyword mapping** (for targeted applications):
1. Extract key requirements from the job description: skills, tools, certifications, competencies, experience.
2. Map each requirement to the candidate's experience.
3. Identify gaps — things the JD asks for that the candidate lacks — and decide how to address them (transferable skills, adjacent experience, or omit).
4. Identify the 8–12 highest-priority keywords to weave throughout the resume.

**Competency mapping** (using FYI framework):
1. Identify which of the 67 FYI competencies the role demands most.
2. Assess the candidate's level (unskilled/skilled/overused) on each.
3. For competencies where the candidate is skilled: highlight with accomplishment statements.
4. For competencies where the candidate is developing: frame as growth trajectory with evidence of progress.

**Positioning:**
- What is the candidate's unique value proposition for *this specific role*?
- What story does the resume tell? (Consistent progression? Turnaround specialist? Technical depth? Leadership breadth?)
- What should the reader remember after 7 seconds of scanning?

### Phase 3: Writing

Write each document using the guidance in Parts II–IV below.

### Phase 4: Styling & Output

Format and produce the final documents using the guidance in Part V and the publishing reference.

## Part II: Resume (CV) Writing

### Resume Structure

**Essential sections** (in order for most candidates):

1. **Header** — Name, contact info (phone, email, LinkedIn, location — city/state only). No photo (in most Western markets). No "References available upon request."

2. **Professional Summary** (3–5 lines) — A dense, high-impact paragraph targeting the specific role. Not an objective statement. Not a generic career overview. This is your 7-second pitch.

   > *"Operations leader with 12 years driving efficiency in manufacturing environments. Reduced production costs by $4.2M at Acme Corp through lean process redesign. Experienced in team leadership (50+ reports), continuous improvement (Six Sigma Black Belt), and ERP implementation. Seeking to bring operational excellence to GlobalCo's VP Operations role."*

3. **Skills / Core Competencies** — A compact keyword block. 8–15 skills matching the JD. Use the exact terms from the job description. This section serves double duty: human scanning and ATS keyword matching.

4. **Professional Experience** — Reverse chronological. For each role:
   - **Company Name** | Location | Dates (month/year)
   - **Job Title**
   - 1-line scope statement (team size, budget, what you were responsible for)
   - 3–6 accomplishment bullets using the formula below

5. **Education** — Degree, institution, graduation year. GPA only if recent and exceptional (>3.5). Relevant coursework only for entry-level.

6. **Certifications & Licenses** — If relevant to the role.

**Optional sections** (include when they strengthen the application):
- Projects (especially for technical roles or career changers)
- Publications / Patents
- Volunteer / Leadership (especially for early-career)
- Languages
- Professional Affiliations

### Accomplishment Statements

The most important skill in resume writing. Every bullet under experience should be an accomplishment, not a duty.

**Formula: Action Verb + What You Did + Measurable Result**

| Weak (Duty) | Strong (Accomplishment) |
|---|---|
| Responsible for managing the sales team | Led 12-person sales team to 140% of annual quota ($8.2M), ranking #1 across 6 regional offices |
| Managed IT projects | Delivered ERP migration 3 weeks ahead of schedule and $200K under budget, serving 2,000 users |
| Handled customer complaints | Redesigned complaint resolution process, reducing average resolution time from 5 days to 8 hours and improving CSAT by 22 points |

**The STAR method for structuring accomplishments:**
- **S**ituation — What was the context?
- **T**ask — What were you asked to do?
- **A**ction — What specifically did you do?
- **R**esult — What was the measurable outcome?

Compress STAR into a single bullet: lead with the action, embed context, end with the result.

**Power verbs by competency type:**

| Competency Area | Power Verbs |
|---|---|
| Leadership | Led, Directed, Oversaw, Managed, Mentored, Built, Coached, Championed |
| Achievement | Delivered, Achieved, Exceeded, Grew, Increased, Generated, Produced |
| Improvement | Reduced, Streamlined, Redesigned, Optimized, Transformed, Automated |
| Creation | Designed, Developed, Created, Launched, Established, Implemented, Architected |
| Analysis | Analysed, Assessed, Evaluated, Identified, Diagnosed, Researched |
| Communication | Presented, Negotiated, Influenced, Persuaded, Facilitated, Authored |

### ATS Optimization

98% of Fortune 500 companies use Applicant Tracking Systems. Your resume must be both machine-readable and human-persuasive.

**ATS-safe formatting rules:**
- Use standard section headings: "Experience," "Education," "Skills" (not creative alternatives)
- Use a single-column layout (no tables, text boxes, or multi-column designs)
- Use standard fonts: Calibri, Arial, Garamond, Georgia, Cambria (10–12pt body, 14–16pt name)
- Use .docx format when submitting to an ATS (DOCX parses more reliably than PDF in most systems)
- No headers/footers for critical content (many ATS skip them)
- No images, icons, charts, or graphics
- Use standard bullet characters (•), not custom symbols

**Keyword strategy:**
- Mirror the exact language of the job description. If the JD says "project management," use "project management" — not "managing projects."
- Include keywords in multiple sections (summary, skills, experience) for higher relevance scoring.
- Use both the full term and the acronym: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)."
- Don't keyword-stuff. Every keyword must be backed by context in the experience section.

## Part III: Cover Letter Writing

### Structure (One Page, 3–4 Paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: The Hook** (2–3 sentences)
State the role you're applying for. Immediately articulate why you're a strong fit. Lead with your most compelling qualification.

> *"I'm writing to apply for the VP Operations role at GlobalCo. With 12 years leading manufacturing operations and a track record of delivering $4M+ in annual cost savings, I bring the operational leadership and continuous improvement expertise your team is seeking."*

**Paragraph 2–3: Evidence** (The body)
Address the top 2–3 requirements from the job description with specific accomplishments. Each point should mirror a key requirement and provide evidence using STAR-compressed format.

> *"Your posting emphasises lean process improvement. At Acme Corp, I led a cross-functional team of 15 to redesign our assembly line workflow, reducing cycle time by 35% and eliminating $1.2M in annual waste. This initiative earned the company's President's Award for operational excellence."*

**Final Paragraph: Close** (2–3 sentences)
Express enthusiasm. Reference what you'd contribute. Invite next steps.

> *"I'm excited about GlobalCo's commitment to sustainable manufacturing and would welcome the chance to discuss how my experience can accelerate your operational transformation. I'm available for a conversation at your convenience."*

### Principles
- **Mirror the JD language.** If the JD says "stakeholder engagement," your cover letter says "stakeholder engagement."
- **Don't repeat the resume.** The cover letter adds narrative, context, and personality the resume can't.
- **Show research.** Reference something specific about the company — a recent initiative, a stated value, a challenge you've read about.
- **Keep it to one page.** 250–400 words.

## Part IV: Selection Criteria Addendum

Many government and institutional roles require a separate document addressing each selection criterion. This is common in Australian Public Service (APS), UK Civil Service, and similar frameworks.

### Structure

**For each criterion**, write 150–300 words using STAR:

```
CRITERION: Demonstrated ability to lead and manage teams in a complex environment.

SITUATION: As Operations Manager at Acme Corp, I led a team of 45 across three
shifts in a 24/7 manufacturing facility undergoing a major ERP transition.

TASK: I was responsible for maintaining production targets while simultaneously
managing the change impact of the new system on frontline staff.

ACTION: I established a change champion network of 8 team leads, designed a
phased training program delivered across all shifts, and held weekly pulse surveys
to identify emerging issues before they escalated. When resistance from the night
shift threatened the go-live timeline, I personally worked night shifts for two
weeks to build trust and address concerns directly.

RESULT: The ERP transition was completed on schedule with zero production days
lost. Staff engagement scores improved by 15 points in the post-implementation
survey. The approach was adopted as the standard change management template for
subsequent technology rollouts across the organisation.
```

### Principles
- **Address every criterion.** Missing one can disqualify you.
- **Use the criterion's exact language** in your response.
- **Quantify results** wherever possible.
- **One example per criterion** is usually sufficient. Make it your best.
- **Link to the FYI competency framework** when identifying which capabilities to demonstrate (e.g., a criterion about "managing in ambiguity" maps to FYI Competency #2).

## Part V: Document Styling and Output

### Professional Formatting Guidelines

**Typography:**
- Name: 16–20pt, bold
- Section headings: 12–14pt, bold, with a subtle line or spacing beneath
- Body text: 10.5–11.5pt
- Consistent margins: 0.5"–1.0" all sides
- Line spacing: 1.0–1.15

**Visual hierarchy:**
- Clear section breaks (whitespace or subtle horizontal rules)
- Bold for company names and job titles
- Consistent date alignment (right-aligned or tab-separated)
- Bullet points for accomplishments (not paragraphs)

**Length:**
- 1 page: Early career (0–5 years)
- 2 pages: Mid to senior career (5–15+ years)
- 3+ pages: Academic CV, executive, or when required by application format
- Cover letter: Always 1 page

### Output Formats

For detailed publishing commands, see `references/resume-formatting.md`.

**PDF (recommended for most submissions):**
```bash
# Via Pandoc (from Markdown)
pandoc resume.md -o resume.pdf --pdf-engine=xelatex \
  -V geometry:margin=0.75in -V fontsize=11pt \
  -V mainfont="Garamond" -V colorlinks=true

# Via Typst (modern, fast, excellent typography)
typst compile resume.typ resume.pdf
```

**DOCX (for ATS submission):**
```bash
pandoc resume.md --reference-doc=resume-template.docx -o resume.docx
```

**HTML (for online portfolios):**
```bash
pandoc resume.md -s --self-contained -c resume.css -o resume.html
```

## Part VI: Revision Checklist

When revising an existing resume, check each item:

- [ ] **Targeting:** Does every section align with the target role? Remove irrelevant content.
- [ ] **Accomplishments:** Is every bullet an accomplishment (not a duty)? Does it have a measurable result?
- [ ] **Keywords:** Are the top 10 JD keywords present? In multiple sections?
- [ ] **Summary:** Does the professional summary immediately convey fit for *this specific role*?
- [ ] **Consistency:** Dates, formatting, tense (past for previous roles, present for current) consistent throughout?
- [ ] **ATS safety:** Single column? Standard headings? Standard font? No tables/graphics?
- [ ] **Length:** Appropriate for career stage? No filler?
- [ ] **Proofread:** Zero typos, grammatical errors, or inconsistencies?
- [ ] **Contact info:** Phone, email, LinkedIn all current and professional?
- [ ] **Cover letter alignment:** Does the cover letter complement (not repeat) the resume?
