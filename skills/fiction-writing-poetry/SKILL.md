This skill guides poetry craft. It provides specific, opinionated technique for writing literary-grade poetry, drawn from the most respected craft pedagogy and practitioner wisdom.

The user provides poetry requirements: a poem to draft, a draft to revise, a form to work in, or output to evaluate. They may be at any stage — first impulse through final polish.

## Core Principle: Compression Is the Art

Poetry is the most compressed form of literary language. Every word must earn its place twice — once for meaning, once for sound. Where prose has paragraphs to develop an idea, a poem has lines. Where prose can afford a decorative sentence, a poem cannot afford a decorative word.

This is not minimalism. A rich, dense, ornate poem is still compressed — every ornament works. Compression means nothing is idle. The poem is language under maximum pressure, and the pressure produces heat, light, and meaning that prose cannot reach.

This principle governs everything that follows. When in doubt, ask: "Is this word doing two jobs?" If only one — or none — cut it or replace it.

## 1. The Line

**The line is the fundamental unit of poetry. It is what makes poetry poetry — not meter, not rhyme, not imagery, but the decision to break language into lines.**

### What the Line Does (Wallace & Boisseau)

The line is a unit of attention. When the reader reaches the end of a line, three things happen:

- **A micro-pause.** Even in enjambed verse, the line-end produces a fractional hesitation — a breath, a beat of attention. This pause is a rhythmic tool.
- **A unit of meaning.** The words on one line form a temporary statement before the next line qualifies, extends, or contradicts it. The line-end meaning and the sentence meaning can differ — and that difference is where much of poetry's power lives.
- **A visual unit.** The line occupies space on the page. Its length, its position, its relationship to the lines around it — these are visual signals.

### Line Breaks

The most important craft decision in free verse is where to break the line. There are two fundamental choices:

**End-stopped line:** The line-end coincides with a syntactic pause (period, comma, clause boundary). The line rests. The meaning settles.

> The art of losing isn't hard to master;
> so many things seem filled with the intent
> to be lost that their loss is no disaster.
> — Elizabeth Bishop

**Enjambed line:** The line breaks mid-syntax — the sentence runs over into the next line. The reader is pulled forward. Enjambment creates tension between the line-unit and the sentence-unit.

> Let the light of late afternoon
> shine through chinks in the barn, moving
> up the bales as the sun moves down.
> — Jane Kenyon

### The Line-Break Test (Maxwell)

At every line-end, ask: what does the reader know at the end of THIS line, before they read the next? If the line-end creates a momentary meaning — a surprise, an ambiguity, a tension — the break is working. If the line-end is arbitrary — breaking mid-phrase for no rhythmic or semantic reason — move it.

Maxwell: "The end of a line is a cliff. The reader steps off into white space — into silence, into not-knowing. What you place at that cliff-edge is what the reader carries into the silence."

### Line Length

- **Short lines** (1–4 words) create speed, urgency, isolation. Each word is magnified. Short-lined poems feel breathless or spare.
- **Medium lines** (5–8 words) are the workhouse of English-language poetry. Enough room for a clause, a thought-unit.
- **Long lines** (9+ words) create sweep, expansiveness, accumulation. Whitman, Ginsberg, C.K. Williams. The long line risks prosaicness — it must justify its length with rhythmic energy.
- **Varied line length** within a poem creates visual and rhythmic texture. Consistent length creates regularity (which formal verse exploits and free verse should be wary of).

### Anti-Patterns

- **Arbitrary line breaks:** Breaking at the margin rather than at a meaningful point. If the line breaks could be moved three words either way with no effect, they are not doing work.
- **Every line end-stopped:** Produces a plodding, list-like quality. Even formal verse needs enjambment for life.
- **Enjambment for its own sake:** Breaking mid-article or mid-preposition ("the / dog") without purpose. Enjambment must create meaning, not just demonstrate knowledge of the technique.

## 2. Sound

**Poetry is an art of sound. Before it means, it sounds. A poem that doesn't work in the ear doesn't work.**

### Meter and Rhythm

**Meter** is a regular pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables. English-language poetry inherits several fundamental meters:

| Meter | Pattern | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Iambic** | da-DUM | "To STRIVE, to SEEK, to FIND, and NOT to YIELD" |
| **Trochaic** | DUM-da | "TELL me NOT in MOURN-ful NUM-bers" |
| **Anapestic** | da-da-DUM | "'Twas the NIGHT be-fore CHRIST-mas" |
| **Dactylic** | DUM-da-da | "THIS is the FOR-est pri-ME-val" |
| **Spondaic** | DUM-DUM | "HEART-BREAK" (used for emphasis, not sustained) |

**Rhythm** is what actually happens in the poem — the living pulse that plays with, against, and around the meter. A perfectly regular iambic pentameter line is metronomic and dead. A line that establishes iambic pentameter and then breaks it at a key moment — that is alive.

**Substitution** is the craft of varying the meter: replacing an expected foot with an unexpected one. A trochaic substitution (DUM-da) at the start of an iambic line creates emphasis. A spondee (DUM-DUM) slows the line down. These departures from the pattern are where emotional meaning lives — the reader feels the regularity disrupted, and the disruption carries weight.

### The Body in the Rhythm (Maxwell)

Maxwell insists that meter is not an abstract pattern but a bodily experience. The iamb mirrors the heartbeat. The line mirrors the breath. Stress is physical — the mouth and throat exert more force on stressed syllables. When you write in meter, you are writing for the body, not just the mind.

Test: can you feel the stresses physically when you read the line aloud? If the stresses are ambiguous or fall in unnatural places, the meter is fighting the language instead of riding it.

### Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Repeated initial consonants. "The fair breeze blew, the white foam flew." Creates connection between words, reinforces emphasis.
- **Assonance:** Repeated vowel sounds within words. "The rain in Spain." Creates internal music, slows or speeds the line depending on the vowel (long vowels slow; short vowels quicken).
- **Consonance:** Repeated consonant sounds within or at the end of words. "Odds and ends." "Struts and frets." Creates texture and cohesion.
- **Rhyme:** Full rhyme (moon/June), slant rhyme (moon/bone), internal rhyme (within a line), eye rhyme (love/move). Full end-rhyme is the most visible sound device; slant rhyme is subtler, more contemporary, and often more interesting.
- **Euphony:** Smooth, pleasant sound — liquid consonants (l, r), open vowels, flowing rhythm. Use for beauty, calm, or seduction.
- **Cacophony:** Harsh, clashing sound — hard consonants (k, t, g), closed vowels, clotted consonant clusters. Use for violence, discomfort, disruption.

### Sound Enacting Meaning (Pound: Melopoeia)

The sound of the poem should enact its subject. This is not mere onomatopoeia — it is the total sonic texture reinforcing the meaning:

- A poem about grief might use long vowels and soft consonants — the mouth enacting slowness, heaviness
- A poem about violence might use hard consonants and short vowels — the mouth enacting impact
- A poem about confusion might pile consonant clusters that resist easy pronunciation

Pound called this *melopoeia* — "wherein the words are charged, over and above their plain meaning, with some musical property, which directs the bearing or trend of that meaning."

### Anti-Patterns

- **Tin-ear meter:** Lines that scan on paper but sound wrong spoken aloud. Fix: read aloud. If you stumble, the meter is fighting the language.
- **Sound without purpose:** Alliterating everything, rhyming compulsively, without the sound serving meaning. Sound devices are tools, not decorations.
- **Forced rhyme:** Distorting syntax or choosing the wrong word to hit a rhyme. If the rhyme sounds forced, the reader hears the mechanism instead of the poem.
- **Monotonous rhythm:** Sustained regularity without variation. Even a sonnet needs rhythmic life — substitution, caesura, enjambment.

## 3. Image & Figure

**The image is poetry's atom — the irreducible unit of perception that makes the reader see, hear, taste, touch, or smell something specific.**

### The Image (Pound: Phanopoeia)

Pound defined the image as "that which presents an intellectual and emotional complex in an instant of time." Not a picture. A complex — thought, feeling, and perception fused into a single unit.

The Imagist rules remain foundational:
1. **Direct treatment of the thing.** No abstractions standing between the reader and the object. Not "beauty" — the specific thing that is beautiful.
2. **No word that does not contribute.** (See: Core Principle.)
3. **Compose in the sequence of the musical phrase, not the metronome.** The image's rhythm is organic, not mechanical.

### Concrete vs. Abstract

Poetry's power is in the concrete. Abstractions (love, death, freedom, beauty, truth) are necessary sometimes — but they must be earned by the concrete images around them. An abstraction that arrives without concrete grounding floats away. A concrete image that embodies the abstraction anchors it in the reader's body.

Not:
> Death is inevitable and comes for all.

But:
> This living hand, now warm and capable
> — Keats

The hand IS mortality, without the word "death" appearing.

### Figurative Language

**Metaphor:** A is B. "All the world's a stage." The poem asserts identity between unlike things, and the reader's mind bridges the gap — the bridging IS the meaning.

**Simile:** A is like B. "My love is like a red, red rose." Simile preserves the separateness of the two things while asserting likeness. Less bold than metaphor, but can be more precise — the "like" invites the reader to consider exactly how A resembles B.

**Metonymy:** A thing stands for a related thing. "The crown" for the monarchy. "Boots on the ground" for soldiers. The relationship is contiguity, not resemblance.

**Synecdoche:** Part for whole. "All hands on deck." "Hired hands." The body part standing for the person reveals something about how the person is seen — as labor, as capability, as flesh.

**Symbol:** An image that accumulates meaning beyond its literal content through the poem's context. Symbols are not codes (rose ≠ love). A symbol emerges when a concrete image is placed under enough pressure by the poem's structure that it begins to radiate meaning. You cannot assign a symbol; you can only create the conditions for one to form.

### The Objective Correlative (Eliot)

An emotion in poetry is not stated — it is evoked by finding "a set of objects, a situation, a chain of events which shall be the formula of that particular emotion." The poet does not say "I am sad." The poet presents the images, objects, and actions that produce sadness in the reader. The reader feels the emotion; the poem provides the correlative.

This is the poetic equivalent of fiction's "render, don't explain."

### Anti-Patterns

- **Abstract opening:** Starting with an abstraction ("Love is..." "Time passes...") before any concrete image has been established. Fix: start with the image. Let the abstraction emerge, if it must appear at all.
- **Decoration:** Figures that ornament but do not illuminate. If the metaphor can be removed without loss of meaning, it is decorative. Fix: the figure should be the meaning, not a gloss on it.
- **Mixed figures:** "The ship of state sailed into a minefield of opposition, but the captain kept his eye on the ball." Each figure cancels the others. Fix: one extended figure or a sequence that tracks.
- **Dead figures:** Clichéd metaphors the reader no longer sees. "Heart of gold," "sea of troubles," "light at the end of the tunnel." Fix: find the specific, strange, never-before-seen comparison.
- **Image pile-up:** So many images in succession that none land. Fix: give each image room. Let the reader see it before you move to the next.

## 4. Form

**Form is not a container the poem is poured into. Form is the body of the poem — the shape its meaning takes.**

### Fixed Forms

Fixed forms are not constraints. They are *productive pressure.* The sonnet's fourteen lines, the villanelle's repetitions, the ghazal's autonomous couplets — these are not obstacles but generators. The form pushes the poet toward discoveries they would not have made in free verse. The constraint is the creativity.

**Key Fixed Forms:**

| Form | Structure | What It Does Well |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Sonnet** | 14 lines, iambic pentameter, volta | Argument, turn, revelation. The volta (the "turn" at line 9 in Petrarchan, or the couplet in Shakespearean) is the sonnet's engine — it forces the poem to shift. |
| **Villanelle** | 19 lines, 2 refrains, 5 tercets + quatrain | Obsession, circularity, the thing that cannot be escaped. The repeating lines gain meaning through changed context. Bishop's "One Art," Thomas's "Do Not Go Gentle." |
| **Sestina** | 39 lines, 6 stanzas of 6 lines + 3-line envoi, end-word rotation | Obsession through variation. The six end-words return in shifting patterns, forcing the poet to find new meanings for the same words. |
| **Ghazal** | Couplets, each autonomous, linked by refrain (radif) and rhyme (qafia) | Fragmentation, desire, the tension between independence and connection. Each couplet is a self-contained poem within the larger poem. |
| **Pantoum** | Quatrains, lines 2 and 4 become lines 1 and 3 of next stanza | Dream-logic, memory, the past intruding on the present. The recycled lines create an eerie, recursive quality. |
| **Haiku** | 3 lines (traditionally 5-7-5 syllables in English, though this is contested) | The instant of perception. Juxtaposition of two images with a cut (kireji) between them. Compression at maximum. |
| **Blank verse** | Unrhymed iambic pentameter | Sustained speech, narrative, dramatic monologue. The flexibility of free verse with the backbone of meter. Shakespeare, Milton, Frost, Stevens. |

### Free Verse

Free verse is not formless verse. Free verse is verse that discovers its own form — each poem creating the structure its content requires, rather than inheriting a pre-existing pattern.

The danger of free verse: without inherited constraints, the poet must generate their own. Bad free verse is prose with line breaks. Good free verse has:

- **An audible rhythm** — not meter, but a pulse. Repetition, variation, cadence.
- **Deliberate line breaks** — every break doing work (see Section 1).
- **Internal structure** — turns, sections, patterns of imagery, recurring sounds.
- **Compression** — no word idle.

Levertov on free verse: "Form is never more than a revelation of content." The poem's form should feel *inevitable* — as if no other shape could hold this particular content.

### Stanza

The stanza is to the poem what the paragraph is to prose — a unit of development — but more. Stanzas also create visual rhythm, white-space patterning, and expectations of regularity or irregularity.

- **Regular stanzas** (tercets, quatrains, etc.) create expectations the poet can fulfill or subvert.
- **Irregular stanzas** create their own rhythm — the eye and ear adjust to the poem's particular logic.
- **Stanza breaks** are silences. What happens in the white space between stanzas — the reader's mind bridging the gap — is part of the poem.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Free verse by default:** Choosing free verse because form feels difficult, not because the poem demands it. Fix: try the poem in a form. If the form resists, free verse is the right choice. If the form generates discoveries, the form is the right choice.
- **Form as costume:** Using a sonnet's fourteen lines and rhyme scheme without its structural logic (the volta). The form is not the shape — it is the shape AND its function. Fix: understand what each form does and use that function.
- **Syllable-counting without ear:** Counting syllables to hit 5-7-5 without attending to the haiku's actual craft (juxtaposition, the cut, compression). Fix: study the Japanese tradition and its best English practitioners.

## 5. Voice & Diction

**The voice of a poem is not the poet's voice. It is the voice the poem needs — the specific diction, syntax, and register that this particular poem demands.**

### Speaker vs. Poet

Every poem has a speaker. The speaker may be close to the poet (lyric "I") or far from the poet (dramatic monologue). Either way, the speaker is a construction — a voice made for this poem's purposes.

The poet's job: find the voice this poem needs, not the voice the poet habitually uses. A poet who always sounds the same is not versatile — they are stuck.

### Diction

**Diction** is word choice — and it is the poet's most granular tool.

- **Register:** Formal, informal, colloquial, archaic, technical, slang. The register establishes the speaker and the poem's relationship to its subject. Mixing registers deliberately (a clinical term in an intimate poem, slang in a formal structure) creates productive friction.
- **Latinate vs. Anglo-Saxon:** English has two vocabularies — the Latinate (polysyllabic, abstract, formal) and the Anglo-Saxon (monosyllabic, concrete, blunt). The greatest effects come from deploying both: a passage of Latinate complexity followed by a monosyllabic Anglo-Saxon line that cuts through it.
- **Denotation and connotation:** The dictionary meaning and the cloud of associations around it. "House" and "home" denote the same thing; they connote entirely different feelings. The poet works in connotation.
- **The right word (Pound):** Not the approximate word. Not the fancy word. Not the first word that came to mind. The word that is exact, that sounds right, that carries the right connotation, that fits the rhythm. Flaubert's *le mot juste* applies to poetry with even more force than to prose, because the poem has no room to recover from the wrong word.

### Logopoeia (Pound)

Pound's third kind of poetry: "the dance of the intellect among words." Logopoeia uses words not just for sound or image but for their intellectual and ironic resonances — playing on the reader's knowledge of how words have been used before, their cultural baggage, their double meanings. This is the province of wit, irony, and linguistic self-awareness.

### Tone

Tone is the poem's emotional register — its attitude toward its subject. Tone can be:
- Reverent, irreverent, ironic, sincere, mournful, ecstatic, detached, intimate, playful, grave
- Consistent or shifting (a poem that moves from irony to sincerity, the shift itself carrying meaning)
- At odds with the content (saying something terrible in a light tone, which is more devastating than saying it gravely)

### Anti-Patterns

- **Poet-voice:** A generic "poetic" register that sounds like Poetry rather than like a specific poem. Heightened diction, vague imagery, emotional generality. Fix: ask — who is speaking? About what? In what circumstances? The answers determine the diction.
- **Tonal flatness:** The poem stays at one emotional register throughout, never shifting, never surprising. Fix: find the poem's turn (see Section 7) and let the tone shift with it.
- **Archaic diction without purpose:** "Thee," "thou," "'twas," "ere," "o'er" — unless the poem is deliberately engaging with a historical voice. Fix: write in the language of now, unless the poem has a specific reason not to.

## 6. Syntax & the Sentence

**In poetry, two structures coexist: the line and the sentence. The tension between them is where much of poetry's energy lives.**

### The Line/Sentence Tension (Wallace & Boisseau)

Prose has sentences. Poetry has sentences AND lines. When these two structures align (end-stopped lines), the poem rests. When they diverge (enjambment), the poem generates energy — the reader is pulled forward by the sentence while the line asks them to pause.

The master-craft of poetry is managing this tension:
- A sentence that spans three lines creates forward momentum
- A sentence that ends mid-line (caesura) creates a pocket of silence within the line
- A fragment that occupies an entire line creates emphasis through isolation
- A long sentence followed by a short one creates the same punch as in prose — amplified by the line structure

### Syntax as Meaning

In prose, syntax is transparent — you read through it to the meaning. In poetry, syntax IS meaning:

- **Inversion** (placing the object before the subject, the adjective after the noun) slows the reader and creates emphasis on the displaced element. "Strange the world is" carries different weight than "The world is strange."
- **Parallelism** (repeating a syntactic structure) creates rhythm and emphasis. "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields." The repetition is the meaning — inexorability, resolve.
- **Fragmentation** (sentences without subjects, verbs, or objects) creates immediacy, urgency, or disorientation. "Morning. Frost on the window. No sound."
- **Delay** (suspending the main verb or object with subordinate clauses) creates tension and anticipation. The reader waits for resolution — and what arrives at the end of the waiting is amplified.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Prose syntax with line breaks:** If the lines could be un-broken into perfectly normal prose sentences with no loss, the poem is not using the line/sentence tension. Fix: find the places where the line and the sentence can work against each other.
- **Tortured syntax for rhyme:** Inverting word order to hit a rhyme-word at the end of a line, when the inversion sounds unnatural. Fix: if the syntax sounds forced, find a different rhyme or restructure the line.
- **All fragments, no sentences:** Fragmentary writing can be powerful, but sustained fragmentation without complete sentences loses the reader's sense of grammar and therefore of meaning. Fix: anchor fragments with occasional complete sentences.

## 7. The Turn (Volta)

**The turn is the most important structural move in a poem. It is the moment where the poem shifts — in direction, in understanding, in tone, in scale. Without a turn, a poem is a statement. With a turn, it is an experience.**

### What Turns (Wallace & Boisseau, Hirsch)

A poem can turn in many ways:

- **Argument to counter-argument:** The Petrarchan sonnet's classic structure — "but" at line 9.
- **Observation to insight:** The poem describes, then understands. The turn is the moment of understanding.
- **Outside to inside:** The poem moves from the external world to the speaker's interior. Or vice versa.
- **Present to past (or future):** A temporal shift that changes the poem's meaning.
- **Particular to universal:** The poem moves from one image to a larger resonance.
- **Tone shift:** Irony to sincerity, lightness to gravity, distance to intimacy.
- **Image shift:** One image-world gives way to another, and the juxtaposition creates meaning.

### Where Turns Live

- In a sonnet, the volta is structurally required — line 9 (Petrarchan) or the final couplet (Shakespearean).
- In free verse, the turn can arrive anywhere — but it must arrive. A poem without a turn is an observation without a discovery.
- In a villanelle, the turn happens through the changing context of the repeated lines — the same words meaning something different each time they return.
- In haiku, the turn is the cut (kireji) between the two juxtaposed images.

### The Turn as the Poem's Reason for Existing

Hirsch: a poem begins because something has happened to the poet — an experience, a perception, a feeling. But the poem becomes art at the turn — the moment the poem moves beyond the initial impulse and discovers something the poet didn't know before writing.

If the poem ends where it began — if the poet knew the conclusion before writing — the poem is an illustration, not a discovery. The turn is where the poem surprises its author.

### Anti-Patterns

- **No turn:** The poem makes one statement, illustrates it, and ends. Fix: find the poem's "but" — the counter-pressure, the complication, the shift. If none exists, the poem may be an observation rather than a poem.
- **Predictable turn:** The reader sees it coming from line 3. Fix: delay the turn, disguise it, or make it more radical.
- **Forced epiphany:** The poem arrives at a Grand Insight in the final lines that the imagery hasn't earned. Fix: the turn must emerge from the poem's own material, not be imposed from outside.

## 8. White Space & Silence

**The poem exists on a page. The page is not neutral. White space is silence, absence, death, the unknown — and the poem defines itself against it.**

### Maxwell's White Space

Maxwell's central insight: white space is the void the poem resists. Every line of a poem is bordered by white space — by silence, by nothing. The poem is an act of assertion against emptiness. This is why line breaks matter: the line ends, and the white space begins, and the reader is momentarily in silence before the next line asserts itself.

"The white page is a void, and into it you throw, you lower, or you tremble forth, your words. The void is the given. The words are the human."

### What White Space Does

- **Between stanzas:** A breath. A silence. The reader's mind works in the gap — connecting, reconsidering, anticipating.
- **At line-ends:** The cliff-edge (Maxwell). The momentary suspension before the next line.
- **Indentation:** Visual displacement — the line moved from where the reader expects it, creating emphasis or disorientation.
- **Short lines surrounded by white space:** Isolation. The words are exposed, unprotected by the density of surrounding language.
- **The end of the poem:** The final descent into white space. The last line must be strong enough to hold against the void that follows it.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Ignoring the page:** Treating the poem as pure sound, forgetting that it is also a visual object. Fix: look at the poem's shape on the page. Does it reflect the poem's movement?
- **Gimmicky layout:** Using visual arrangement as a substitute for verbal power. Fix: concrete poetry is a valid tradition, but most poems earn their visual structure through language first, layout second.

## 9. Revision

**A first draft is a conversation with yourself. A revised poem is a conversation with the reader. The distance between them is where craft lives.**

### The Revision Hierarchy for Poetry

Revise in this order (macro → micro):

1. **Necessity:** Does this poem need to exist? Does it discover something? Does it have a turn? If not, it may be an exercise, not a poem — and that is fine. Learn from it and move on.
2. **Structure:** Is the form right? Is the turn in the right place? Are the stanzas doing work? Is the poem the right length — or is it a 12-line poem hiding inside 30 lines?
3. **Image:** Are the images concrete, specific, and fresh? Any abstractions that could be replaced with images? Any dead metaphors?
4. **Sound:** Read aloud. Does the poem have music? Are the sound devices serving meaning? Is the rhythm alive or metronomic?
5. **Line:** Is every line break doing work? Test each break: what does the reader know at the line-end? Is the enjambment meaningful?
6. **Diction:** Is every word the right word? Any approximations? Any vocabulary inflation? Any clichés? Any words that could be cut?
7. **The last line:** Does the poem end, or does it just stop? Is the last line strong enough to hold against the white space that follows? (Oliver: "The end of the poem is the most important part.")

### The Compression Pass

After structural revision, do a compression pass:
- Remove every word that doesn't do at least two jobs (meaning + sound, meaning + rhythm, meaning + image)
- Remove every adjective that is predictable (blue sky, hot sun, cold snow)
- Remove every line that merely repeats what a previous line already established
- Remove every abstraction that an image already conveys
- If the poem gets shorter by 30%, it is probably getting better

### The Sound Pass

Read the poem aloud — physically, not mentally:
- Where do you stumble? The rhythm is fighting the language. Fix it.
- Where do you speed up? The poem wants to move there — is that right?
- Where do you slow down? The poem wants to rest there — is that right?
- Where does the music die? Find the flat passage and give it sonic life.
- Are any rhymes forced? Any alliterations calling attention to themselves?

### The Image Pass

For every image:
- Is it concrete? Can the reader see/hear/taste/touch/smell it?
- Is it specific? Not "a tree" but what kind? Not "a bird" but which one?
- Is it fresh? Has this image been used in ten thousand other poems? If so, find the version that only this poem could use.
- Is it earned? Does it arise from the poem's world, or is it imported for decoration?

### Anti-Patterns in Revision

- **Tinkering instead of cutting:** Spending hours on word substitutions when the real problem is structural — the poem needs a stanza removed or a turn restructured. Fix: macro before micro.
- **Falling in love with a line:** Keeping a beautiful line that doesn't serve the poem. Fix: save it in a notebook. Use it in a poem where it belongs.
- **Revising the life out:** Polishing until every edge is smooth and every surprise is gone. Fix: preserve the poem's strangeness. The thing that feels slightly wrong may be the thing that makes it alive.

## 10. The AI Anti-Pattern Checklist

Before finalising any poetry output, verify NONE of these are present:

- [ ] **Named emotions:** No "I felt sadness," "joy filled me," "anger rose." Find the objective correlative — the image, action, or object that produces the emotion in the reader.
- [ ] **Greeting-card sentiment:** No pre-digested emotional conclusions. "Love conquers all," "time heals all wounds," "we are all connected." These are slogans, not poems. The poem must discover its own conclusions.
- [ ] **Generic imagery:** No "the stars shone bright," "the waves crashed on the shore," "the wind whispered." These images are invisible — the reader sees nothing because the images have been used ten million times. Find the specific, strange, never-before-seen image.
- [ ] **Forced rhyme:** No distorted syntax or wrong-word-for-right-rhyme. If the rhyme draws attention to itself as mechanical, it has failed.
- [ ] **Metronomic rhythm:** No sustained perfect regularity without variation. Even formal verse needs rhythmic life — substitution, caesura, enjambment.
- [ ] **Abstract opening:** No beginning with a philosophical statement. Start with the concrete — image, action, voice. Let abstraction emerge.
- [ ] **Predictable structure:** No pattern so regular it becomes wallpaper (A-B-A-B rhyme with no variation, every stanza four lines, every line the same length). Unless the regularity itself is the point, vary.
- [ ] **Epiphany inflation:** No grand revelation in the final lines that the poem hasn't earned. The turn must emerge from the poem's own material.
- [ ] **Poet-voice:** No generic "poetic" register — heightened, vague, emotionally general. The poem must sound like THIS poem, not like Poetry.
- [ ] **Filler words:** No words present only for meter or syllable count ("very," "really," "just," "so," "quite"). Every word must earn its place.
- [ ] **Explaining the image:** No lines that tell the reader what an image "means." If the image needs explaining, it is the wrong image.
- [ ] **Ornamental figures:** No metaphors or similes that decorate without illuminating. Each figure must make the reader see something they couldn't see without it.
- [ ] **Symmetrical stanzas without purpose:** No identical stanza lengths unless the form demands it or the regularity serves meaning.
- [ ] **Weak endings:** No poems that trail off, summarise, or explain themselves at the end. The last line must hold against the white space. It must be a door, not a wall.

## Working Method

When this skill is loaded for a poetry project, apply it as follows:

### During Drafting

- **Start with the image, the voice, or the rhythm — not the meaning.** The poem will find its meaning through the writing. If you know the conclusion before you begin, you are writing an essay.
- **Find the line first.** Before anything else, determine the line — its length, its rhythm, its relationship to the sentence. The line IS the poem's form.
- **Write toward the turn.** The poem needs to go somewhere it didn't start. Write until you find the shift — then build the poem around it.
- **Read aloud as you draft.** The ear catches what the eye misses. If a line doesn't sound right, it isn't right — regardless of what it means.
- **Default to concrete.** Start with what can be seen, heard, tasted, touched, smelled. Let abstractions arrive late, if at all.
- **Let the form discover itself** (free verse) or **let the form pressure you** (fixed form). Either way, form is not applied after the fact — it is present from the first line.
- **Track the poem's sonic palette.** What vowels and consonants dominate? Are they serving the meaning? Adjust as you draft.

### During Revision / Evaluation

- Apply the revision hierarchy (necessity → structure → image → sound → line → diction → last line)
- Do the compression pass: every word doing two jobs
- Do the sound pass: read aloud, listen for music and stumbles
- Do the image pass: concrete, specific, fresh, earned
- Check the line breaks: every break doing work
- Run the AI anti-pattern checklist
- Test the turn: does the poem shift? Does it discover something?
- Test the ending: can the last line hold against the white space?

### Evaluating Existing Poems

When asked to evaluate a poem, apply this framework:

1. **What is the poem's occasion?** What caused this poem to exist?
2. **Where is the turn?** What shifts? If nothing shifts, the poem may need one.
3. **Is the imagery concrete and fresh?** Or generic and decorative?
4. **Does the sound serve the meaning?** Read aloud — does the music work?
5. **Are the line breaks doing work?** Test each break for line-end meaning.
6. **Is the diction specific?** The right word, not the approximate word?
7. **Is the form earned?** Free verse: does the poem create its own structure? Fixed form: does it use the form's function, not just its shape?
8. **Does the ending hold?** Is the last line strong enough to be the last thing the reader hears?
9. **What would make this poem better?** Be specific. Point to lines, images, sounds.
