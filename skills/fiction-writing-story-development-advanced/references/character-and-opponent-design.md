# Character Web and Opponent Design: Deep Reference

This reference expands the character and opponent design principles in SKILL.md. Read this when designing characters for a complex narrative, building antagonists, or diagnosing why a story's conflict feels thin.

---

## Building Opponents Who Are Heroes of Their Own Stories

The most common failure in storytelling is a weak opponent. Weak opponents produce weak stories because the protagonist is never genuinely tested. The fix is always the same: make the opponent the hero of their own story.

### The Opponent Design Process

1. **Give the opponent their own premise.** They believe something about the world — passionately, articulately, from experience. Their belief directly contradicts the protagonist's. Both beliefs are partially true.

2. **Give the opponent their own wound.** They didn't arrive at their worldview randomly. Something happened that made them who they are. Understanding their wound creates empathy, even for a villain.

3. **Give the opponent their own arc.** They change (or refuse to change) through the story. Their trajectory is the mirror of the protagonist's — where the protagonist grows, the opponent calcifies, or vice versa.

4. **Make the opponent more powerful.** Not necessarily physically — but in terms of resources, knowledge, position, will, or moral certainty. The protagonist should be outmatched in at least one critical dimension. If the protagonist is stronger, the story loses tension.

5. **Make the opponent right.** About something. The protagonist's weakness should be something the opponent can see clearly. The opponent's critique of the protagonist should be accurate — the protagonist (and the reader) should be unable to dismiss it.

### The Opponent's Attack Plan

The opponent should apply pressure to the protagonist's specific weakness:

- If the protagonist's flaw is pride, the opponent should create situations that tempt or expose that pride
- If the protagonist's flaw is cowardice, the opponent should force situations requiring courage
- If the protagonist's flaw is dishonesty, the opponent should create situations where the truth cannot be avoided

This is **targeted opposition** — the opponent is the protagonist's worst possible adversary because they attack exactly where the protagonist is weakest.

### Types of Opposition

**Direct opponent:** Openly pursues a competing goal. Creates visible, dramatic conflict.

**Indirect opponent:** Pursues the same goal but by different means. The conflict is about method, not objective — which creates moral complexity.

**Internal opponent:** The protagonist's own flaw, fear, or wound operating as self-sabotage. In literary fiction, this is often the primary opponent.

**Systemic opponent:** An institution, a society, a set of rules that constrains the protagonist. Not a person but a force. The protagonist must identify and confront the system's representatives.

**Absent opponent:** The opponent is dead, gone, or unknown — but their influence shapes the protagonist's world. Ghost stories, inheritance plots, and many literary novels use the absent opponent.

## The Character Web in Full

### Design Principles

Every character in the web serves a **dramatic function** — they exist because they pressure the protagonist in a way no other character does. Redundant characters weaken the web.

**The function audit:** For each character, complete: "This character exists because they force the protagonist to ___." If you can't complete that sentence, the character may be redundant.

### Web Positions

**Protagonist:** The character who changes most (or most consequentially refuses to change). They carry the story's central question.

**Main opponent:** The character who most directly opposes the protagonist's goal. They carry the counter-argument.

**Fake-ally opponent (Truby):** Appears to be on the protagonist's side but works against them. Creates the betrayal beat and tests the protagonist's ability to judge character. Often the most dramatically interesting character in the web.

**Ally:** Genuinely supports the protagonist but from a different perspective. The ally's function is to provide an alternative viewpoint that challenges the protagonist's assumptions without opposing their goal. The best allies disagree with the protagonist about *how*, not *whether*.

**Subplot protagonist:** Carries a secondary storyline that tests the premise from a different angle. They face an analogous dilemma and resolve it differently — their outcome comments on the protagonist's.

**Mentor (Vogler):** Provides knowledge, tools, or motivation the protagonist needs. The mentor's limitation is critical — what they cannot teach is what the protagonist must learn alone.

**The reflection character:** Mirrors the protagonist — similar situation, different choice. Shows the protagonist (and the reader) what could have been. The reflection character's fate is the protagonist's alternative future.

### Orchestration for Maximum Friction (Egri)

Egri's orchestration principle: characters must be arranged so that every combination produces conflict or meaningful contrast.

**The orchestration test:** Take any two characters in the web and put them in a room. Does conflict arise naturally from who they are? If two characters can have a pleasant, conflict-free conversation, they are not properly orchestrated.

**Practical technique:** Create a matrix of character pairs. For each pair, identify the source of friction. If any pair has no natural friction, either cut one character or redesign one to create opposition.

## The Four Types of Conflict

Egri identifies four layers of conflict that should operate simultaneously in any well-structured story:

### 1. Static Conflict
Two characters in opposition from the beginning with no possibility of agreement. The conflict is positional — neither will move. This is the weakest form of conflict when used alone because it cannot escalate. Useful as a background tension.

### 2. Jumping Conflict
Conflict that leaps from mild to extreme without gradation. Creates melodrama — the characters overreact. Common in poorly structured stories where the writer hasn't built the incremental escalation. Fix: add intermediate stages.

### 3. Slowly Rising Conflict
Conflict that builds incrementally through a series of confrontations, each more intense than the last. This is the ideal dramatic progression — Egri's "bone structure." Each confrontation reveals more, demands more, and costs more.

### 4. Foreshadowing Conflict
Conflict that is anticipated before it arrives — the audience senses the collision coming. Foreshadowing creates dread, anticipation, or dramatic irony. Most powerful when combined with slowly rising conflict.

**The ideal:** A story uses slowly rising conflict as its backbone, with foreshadowing conflict to build anticipation of key confrontations, and avoids both static and jumping conflict except where deliberately chosen for effect.

## The Self-Revelation Scene

The self-revelation (anagnorisis) is one of the hardest scenes to write because it must feel both surprising and inevitable. It is the moment the protagonist sees themselves truly.

### Architecture of Self-Revelation

1. **Preparation:** The entire story has been building evidence the protagonist hasn't seen (or has refused to see). The reader may or may not be ahead of the character.

2. **Trigger:** Something happens in the crisis or climax that makes the old self-understanding impossible. Often the opponent's action, or the consequences of the protagonist's own action, strips away the last defence.

3. **The moment:** The protagonist sees. This must be rendered in the specific — not "she understood everything" but the precise thing she understood, the precise lie she'd been telling, the precise cost she'd been ignoring.

4. **The response:** What the protagonist does with the revelation. In positive arcs, they change. In tragedy, they cannot. In ironic stories, the change comes with a cost that qualifies the victory.

### Common Failures

- **The speech:** The protagonist explains their revelation in dialogue. This is almost always a mistake. Self-revelation should be demonstrated through action, not articulated through words.
- **The epiphany without preparation:** The revelation arrives as if from nowhere. The reader doesn't feel it was earned because the evidence wasn't planted. Fix: go back and seed earlier scenes with details the protagonist couldn't see but the reader can, in retrospect, recognise.
- **The complete transformation:** The character goes from fully flawed to fully healed in one moment. This feels false. Real change is partial, costly, and incomplete. The protagonist overcomes their main weakness but may acquire a new limitation. The resolution acknowledges this complexity.

## True Character Under Pressure (McKee)

McKee's distinction between characterisation and true character is one of the most useful diagnostic tools in story craft.

**Characterisation:** The observable surface — how someone dresses, speaks, moves, presents themselves to the world. This is the mask.

**True character:** What someone does when the stakes are real and the choice is hard. This is the soul.

**The revelation mechanism:** True character is revealed ONLY under pressure. A character who claims to be brave has characterisation. A character who acts bravely when it costs them everything has true character. The two may contradict — and that contradiction IS the character's dimension.

### Building Pressure for Revelation

Each act should present a **higher-stakes version of the same fundamental test:**

- **Act I pressure:** Low-stakes situation reveals the character's default behaviour. The audience sees the characterisation and begins to sense the gap.
- **Act II pressure:** Medium-stakes situations force the character to choose between their professed values and their self-interest. The gap between characterisation and true character becomes visible.
- **Act III pressure:** Maximum-stakes situation strips away everything except the essential self. True character is revealed — for better or worse.

The character's arc is the story of this progressive revelation, from surface to depth, from mask to soul.
