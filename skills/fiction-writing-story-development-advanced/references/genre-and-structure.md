# Genre Story Types and Structural Conventions: Deep Reference

This reference expands the genre principles in SKILL.md. Read this when working with genre fiction, diagnosing why a story feels like it's missing something, or designing stories that fulfill (or deliberately subvert) audience expectations.

---

## Genre as Promise

Every genre type makes a **structural promise** to the audience. The promise is not about content (there will be a murder, there will be a love interest) but about **pattern** — the kind of journey the story will take, the kind of satisfaction it will deliver, the kind of questions it will explore.

When a story fulfills its genre promise, the audience feels satisfied even if surprised. When a story breaks its genre promise without providing something equally compelling in its place, the audience feels cheated.

Understanding genre deeply means understanding what you're promising — and then either delivering on that promise masterfully, or subverting it in a way that gives the audience something better than what they expected.

## Detailed Story Types (Snyder)

### Monster in the House

**Core promise:** A sin creates a monster; trapped characters must survive.

**Required elements:**
- **The sin:** Someone has done something to invite the monster. Without the sin, the monster is random and the story has no moral dimension.
- **The house:** A confined space — physical or social. The characters cannot simply leave. The confinement creates escalating pressure.
- **The monster:** Must be genuinely terrifying and progressively more powerful/dangerous. The monster should embody or punish the sin.
- **Escalating body count / consequences:** The monster's threat must increase. Each encounter is worse than the last.

**Structural arc:** Discovery of the monster → failed attempts to contain it → revelation of the sin → confrontation requiring acknowledgement of the sin → defeat of the monster (or not, in horror).

**Literary applications:** Any story where characters are trapped with a destructive force they've invited — a toxic relationship, a corrupt institution, a secret that is destroying a family from within.

### Golden Fleece

**Core promise:** A journey in pursuit of a prize; the journey transforms the traveler.

**Required elements:**
- **The road:** Physical or metaphorical travel through varied terrain. Each stage of the journey presents a different challenge.
- **The team:** Fellow travelers who bring conflict, perspective, and companionship. The team's dynamics ARE the story — the fleece is just the destination.
- **The prize:** What the protagonist seeks. The crucial convention: the prize is either not what they expected, or the real prize is what they learned on the road.
- **Transformation through encounter:** Each stop on the journey changes the protagonist. The person who arrives is not the person who left.

**Structural arc:** Departure → road encounters (escalating) → team formation/friction → approach to the prize → discovery that the real prize is internal → return (transformed).

**Literary applications:** Any story of quest or search — physical journey, investigation, pilgrimage, coming-of-age travel.

### Out of the Bottle

**Core promise:** A wish is granted; its consequences reveal a truth.

**Required elements:**
- **The wish:** Something the protagonist desperately wants. The wish must be specific and relate to a genuine need (even if the protagonist misidentifies the need).
- **The fun:** Initial delight in the wish's fulfillment. The audience enjoys the fantasy alongside the protagonist.
- **The cost:** The wish creates escalating problems. What seemed like a gift becomes a trap.
- **The lesson:** The wish was wrong — what the protagonist actually needs is different from what they wished for. The story's resolution requires giving up the wish.

**Structural arc:** Desire → wish granted → fun and games → escalating consequences → attempt to undo → sacrifice of the wish → new understanding.

**Literary applications:** Stories about getting what you thought you wanted — sudden wealth, sudden power, a prayer answered, a fantasy realized.

### Dude with a Problem

**Core promise:** An ordinary person faces extraordinary circumstances and discovers hidden resources.

**Required elements:**
- **The innocent hero:** Someone unprepared for what's coming. Not a trained professional but an ordinary person in over their head.
- **The sudden event:** An external disruption that is not the protagonist's fault. They didn't invite this.
- **Life-or-death stakes:** Literal or figurative. The threat must be extreme and immediate.
- **Resourcefulness over power:** The hero wins not through strength but through cleverness, determination, or moral courage.

**Structural arc:** Normal life → sudden disruption → escalating threat → hero outmatched → discovery of inner resource → confrontation → survival (with transformation).

### Rites of Passage

**Core promise:** Life forces a painful transition; the protagonist must accept what they cannot change.

**Required elements:**
- **The life problem:** Something universal and unavoidable — aging, loss, disillusionment, the end of innocence, the death of a dream.
- **The wrong way:** The protagonist's initial response is to resist, deny, or circumvent the problem. This makes things worse.
- **The painful truth:** The problem cannot be solved — it can only be accepted. The acceptance IS the transformation.
- **The cost:** Acceptance requires giving something up — an illusion, a relationship, a version of themselves.

**Structural arc:** Life crisis → denial/resistance → escalating consequences of resistance → confrontation with the unavoidable → acceptance → diminished but wiser.

**Literary applications:** This is the most common type in literary fiction. Nearly every literary novel about loss, identity, marriage, mortality, or disillusionment follows this pattern.

### Buddy Love

**Core promise:** Two people who need each other resist, connect, are tested, and find completion.

**Required elements:**
- **Complementary incompleteness:** Each character lacks what the other has. Alone, they are half-people.
- **Initial resistance or conflict:** They don't start as friends. Something keeps them apart — personality, circumstances, social position.
- **The midpoint surrender:** They stop fighting the connection.
- **The dark moment:** Something threatens to destroy the bond. One (or both) must choose the relationship over self-interest.

**Structural arc:** Meeting → friction → gradual connection → surrender → complication → dark moment → sacrifice → union (or, in tragic versions, permanent separation).

### Whydunit

**Core promise:** An investigation exposes a dark truth about the world (not just the crime).

**Required elements:**
- **The crime:** A specific wrong that needs to be understood (not just solved).
- **The dark turn:** The investigation reveals that the crime implicates more than the perpetrator — it exposes systemic corruption, institutional failure, or universal human weakness.
- **The detective is changed:** The investigator cannot remain detached. What they discover forces a personal reckoning.

**Structural arc:** Crime → investigation → escalating revelations → the dark turn (implicates the wider world) → personal cost to the detective → resolution that is satisfying but disturbing.

**Literary applications:** Any story driven by investigation — not just crime fiction but stories about uncovering family secrets, institutional corruption, historical truth.

### The Fool Triumphant

**Core promise:** The underdog wins by being what the establishment cannot value.

**Required elements:**
- **The establishment:** A powerful, rigid system that underestimates the fool.
- **The fool's power:** Not conventional strength but innocence, honesty, kindness, or a perspective the establishment lacks.
- **The insider ally:** Someone within the establishment who recognises the fool's value.
- **The triumph:** The fool wins not by becoming like the establishment but by remaining who they are.

### Institutionalized

**Core promise:** A group, its rules, and the cost of belonging or leaving.

**Required elements:**
- **The institution:** A family, a company, a military unit, a religion, a social class — any group with rules.
- **The branding:** The moment the protagonist accepts (or is forced to accept) membership. What they gain and what they give up.
- **The company man:** A character who has fully surrendered to the institution. They show the protagonist what total commitment costs.
- **The choice:** Stay and submit, or leave and lose everything the institution provides.

**Literary applications:** Extremely common in literary fiction — novels about families, marriages, communities, workplaces, and social groups.

### Superhero

**Core promise:** A special person bears the burden of their difference.

**Required elements:**
- **The power:** Something that sets the protagonist apart — talent, knowledge, ability, status, or literal superpowers.
- **The nemesis:** Someone who specifically targets the protagonist because of their power.
- **The curse:** The power comes with a cost. The thing that makes them special also isolates, burdens, or endangers them.
- **The people:** Those the hero protects, who may not understand or appreciate the sacrifice.

## Combining Types

Most complex stories operate within two or more genre types simultaneously:

- A thriller (Dude with a Problem) with a love story (Buddy Love) subplot
- A literary novel that is primarily Rites of Passage with an Institutionalized secondary structure
- A Golden Fleece main plot with a Whydunit investigation driving the road encounters

When combining, identify which type is **primary** (determines the main structure) and which is **secondary** (operates through subplot or subtext). The primary type's conventions must be satisfied; the secondary type's can be abbreviated or adapted.

## Subverting Genre

Genre subversion works only when the audience can feel the convention that's being subverted. The ghost of the expected pattern must be present for the deviation to have meaning.

**Effective subversion:**
- Fulfills the genre promise at a deeper level than expected (the crime is solved, but the solution is worse than the mystery)
- Denies the expected resolution and provides something more honest (the lovers don't reunite, and the story is better for it)
- Shifts genre types mid-story (begins as Golden Fleece, becomes Rites of Passage when the quest turns out to be irrelevant)

**Ineffective subversion:**
- Simply fails to deliver the genre promise without providing an alternative
- Subverts for surprise rather than meaning
- Breaks conventions the audience doesn't know exist (if they don't feel the absence, the subversion is invisible)
