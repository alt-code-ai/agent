# Fallacy Library

A comprehensive catalogue of logical fallacies for use in critiquing and writing persuasive texts. Each entry provides the technical name, common/lay name(s), a concise definition, an illustrative example, and a diagnostic question to help identify the fallacy in practice.

Fallacies are organised into six categories:
1. [Fallacies of Relevance](#1-fallacies-of-relevance) — Premises fail to connect to the conclusion
2. [Fallacies of Presumption](#2-fallacies-of-presumption) — The argument smuggles in unwarranted assumptions
3. [Fallacies of Ambiguity](#3-fallacies-of-ambiguity) — Language shifts meaning within the argument
4. [Formal Fallacies](#4-formal-fallacies) — The logical structure is invalid
5. [Causal and Inductive Fallacies](#5-causal-and-inductive-fallacies) — Errors in reasoning about evidence, cause, and generalisation
6. [Rhetorical and Procedural Fallacies](#6-rhetorical-and-procedural-fallacies) — Argumentative moves that undermine fair discourse

## 1. Fallacies of Relevance

These fallacies occur when the premises, even if true, do not bear on the truth of the conclusion. The reasoning is irrelevant to the point at issue.

### Ad Hominem (Abusive)
- **Common names:** Personal attack, name-calling, character attack
- **Definition:** Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. The arguer's character, background, or motives are used to dismiss their claim.
- **Example:** "You can't trust Dr. Lee's research on climate change — she once got a parking ticket."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the criticism directed at the *person* rather than the *argument*? Would the argument be equally strong or weak regardless of who made it?
- **Note:** Relevant when credibility is genuinely at issue (e.g., a known fabricator's testimony). Fallacious when used to avoid engaging with the argument's substance.

### Ad Hominem (Circumstantial)
- **Common names:** Conflict of interest attack, bias charge, "you would say that"
- **Definition:** Arguing that someone's position can be dismissed because of their circumstances, interests, or affiliations — rather than addressing the evidence they present.
- **Example:** "Of course the oil executive opposes carbon taxes — it would hurt his profits."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the circumstance actually invalidate the evidence and reasoning, or is it merely a reason the person *might* be biased?

### Ad Hominem (Tu Quoque)
- **Common names:** "You too," "look who's talking," appeal to hypocrisy
- **Definition:** Dismissing an argument because the arguer's behaviour is inconsistent with their claim. The arguer's hypocrisy does not make their argument false.
- **Example:** "You tell me to exercise more, but you never go to the gym yourself."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the arguer's inconsistent behaviour actually affect the truth of their claim?

### Appeal to Force (Argumentum ad Baculum)
- **Common names:** Scare tactics, threat, intimidation, might makes right
- **Definition:** Using threats or coercion in place of evidence or reasoning.
- **Example:** "If you don't support this policy, you'll find it very difficult to get promoted."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the "reason" offered actually a threat or unpleasant consequence rather than evidence for the claim's truth?

### Appeal to Pity (Argumentum ad Misericordiam)
- **Common names:** Sympathy play, sob story, emotional blackmail
- **Definition:** Using pity or sympathy as the basis for accepting a conclusion, when pity is irrelevant to the truth of the claim.
- **Example:** "I know I failed the exam, but you should give me a passing grade — my grandmother is ill and I've been under a lot of stress."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the suffering described relevant to the truth of the conclusion? (It may be relevant to a *different* conclusion — e.g., an extension — but not to the one offered.)

### Appeal to Popularity (Argumentum ad Populum)
- **Common names:** Bandwagon, mob appeal, "everyone thinks so," appeal to the masses
- **Definition:** Arguing that something is true or good because many people believe or do it.
- **Example:** "Millions of people use this supplement, so it must work."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the number of people who believe something constitute evidence for its truth? (Sometimes it does — e.g., widespread expert consensus. Often it doesn't.)

### Appeal to Tradition (Argumentum ad Antiquitatem)
- **Common names:** "That's how it's always been done," appeal to custom, traditional wisdom
- **Definition:** Arguing that something is correct or preferable because it is traditional or long-established.
- **Example:** "We've always handled disciplinary issues informally. There's no need for a formal process."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the age or longevity of a practice constitute evidence for its correctness or value?

### Appeal to Novelty (Argumentum ad Novitatem)
- **Common names:** "It's new so it must be better," appeal to progress
- **Definition:** Arguing that something is superior simply because it is new or modern.
- **Example:** "This new management approach must be better — it's cutting-edge."
- **Diagnostic:** Is newness itself evidence of superiority, or is independent evidence needed?

### Appeal to Nature (Argumentum ad Naturam)
- **Common names:** Naturalistic fallacy (informal sense), "it's natural so it's good"
- **Definition:** Arguing that something is good because it is "natural" or bad because it is "unnatural."
- **Example:** "Herbal remedies are better than pharmaceuticals because they're natural."
- **Diagnostic:** Is "naturalness" relevant evidence for the quality or safety being claimed?

### Appeal to Authority (Argumentum ad Verecundiam)
- **Common names:** Expert says so, celebrity endorsement, authority worship
- **Definition:** Using an authority's opinion as evidence when the authority is not a legitimate expert on the relevant topic, or when legitimate experts disagree significantly.
- **Example:** "A famous actor endorses this diet plan, so it must be scientifically sound."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the authority cited a genuine expert in the relevant field? Are they speaking within their area of expertise? Do other experts agree? Are they free from relevant conflicts of interest?
- **Note:** Appeal to authority is *not* always fallacious. Citing a genuine, unconflicted expert in their field of expertise is a legitimate form of evidence. The fallacy occurs when the authority is irrelevant, unqualified, conflicted, or when expert opinion is treated as infallible.

### Appeal to Ignorance (Argumentum ad Ignorantiam)
- **Common names:** "You can't prove it wrong," absence of evidence argument, burden-shifting
- **Definition:** Arguing that something is true because it hasn't been proven false, or that it is false because it hasn't been proven true.
- **Example:** "No one has ever proven that ghosts don't exist, so they must be real."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the argument treating absence of evidence as positive evidence? Where does the burden of proof properly lie?

### Red Herring (Ignoratio Elenchi)
- **Common names:** Distraction, changing the subject, misdirection, smokescreen
- **Definition:** Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue.
- **Example:** "Why should we worry about the company's accounting irregularities? Our employee satisfaction scores are the highest in the industry."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the point raised actually address the issue under discussion, or does it shift attention to something else?

### Straw Man
- **Common names:** Misrepresentation, distortion, attacking a caricature
- **Definition:** Distorting or oversimplifying an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack, then defeating the distorted version.
- **Example:** Opponent says: "We should have stricter regulations on industrial emissions." Straw man response: "My opponent wants to shut down all factories and destroy the economy."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the rebuttal address the *actual* argument, or a weakened or exaggerated version of it?

### Genetic Fallacy
- **Common names:** Origin fallacy, poisoning the well
- **Definition:** Evaluating an argument based on its origin rather than its content and reasoning.
- **Example:** "That idea came from a think tank funded by the industry, so it must be wrong."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the argument being evaluated on its merits, or merely on where it came from?

### Appeal to Consequences (Argumentum ad Consequentiam)
- **Common names:** Wishful thinking, "it would be terrible if true"
- **Definition:** Arguing that a belief is true or false based on the desirability of its consequences, rather than on evidence.
- **Example:** "Climate change can't be as bad as they say — if it were, our entire way of life would have to change."
- **Diagnostic:** Are the consequences of a claim being used as evidence for or against its truth?

## 2. Fallacies of Presumption

These fallacies involve arguments that smuggle in unwarranted assumptions or present choices dishonestly.

### Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)
- **Common names:** Circular reasoning, assuming the conclusion, tautological argument
- **Definition:** The conclusion of the argument is assumed (explicitly or implicitly) in one of the premises.
- **Example:** "Free speech is important because people should be able to say what they want."
- **Diagnostic:** If you removed the conclusion, would the premise still be independently supportable? Or does the premise just restate the conclusion in different words?

### False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)
- **Common names:** Either/or fallacy, black-and-white thinking, excluded middle, bifurcation
- **Definition:** Presenting only two options when more exist. Forces a choice between extremes while ignoring middle ground or alternatives.
- **Example:** "You're either with us or against us."
- **Diagnostic:** Are there really only two options? What alternatives have been excluded?

### Loaded Question (Complex Question)
- **Common names:** Leading question, trick question, "have you stopped...?"
- **Definition:** Asking a question that presupposes something unproven. Any direct answer seems to confirm the presupposition.
- **Example:** "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?"
- **Diagnostic:** Does the question contain a built-in assumption that has not been established?

### False Cause (Non Causa Pro Causa)
- **Common names:** Wrong cause, mistaken cause
- **Definition:** Incorrectly identifying a cause for an event or phenomenon. (See also the more specific causal fallacies in Section 5.)
- **Example:** "The rooster crows before sunrise, so the rooster's crowing causes the sun to rise."
- **Diagnostic:** Is there actual evidence establishing a causal connection, or just a co-occurrence?

### Slippery Slope
- **Common names:** Thin end of the wedge, domino argument, camel's nose, parade of horribles
- **Definition:** Asserting that one event will inevitably lead to a chain of increasingly extreme consequences, without evidence for each link in the chain.
- **Example:** "If we allow students to redo one assignment, soon they'll expect to redo every assignment, then they'll expect to pass without doing any work at all."
- **Diagnostic:** Is each step in the chain supported by evidence? Or is the progression merely asserted as inevitable?
- **Note:** Not every chain-of-consequences argument is fallacious. The argument is legitimate if each link is supported by evidence. It is fallacious when the links are merely assumed.

### Complex Cause (Oversimplified Cause)
- **Common names:** Causal oversimplification, single cause fallacy, reductionism
- **Definition:** Attributing an outcome to a single cause when it results from multiple interacting causes.
- **Example:** "The financial crisis was caused by greedy bankers." (The crisis resulted from regulatory failure, monetary policy, consumer behaviour, financial innovation, and more.)
- **Diagnostic:** Is the identified cause the *only* relevant cause, or one among several?

### Composition
- **Common names:** Part-to-whole fallacy
- **Definition:** Assuming that what is true of the parts must be true of the whole.
- **Example:** "Every player on this team is outstanding, so this must be an outstanding team." (Outstanding individuals don't always make an outstanding team — chemistry, coordination, and strategy matter too.)
- **Diagnostic:** Does the property in question transfer from parts to whole? (Some do — e.g., "every brick is red, so the wall is red." Many don't.)

### Division
- **Common names:** Whole-to-part fallacy
- **Definition:** Assuming that what is true of the whole must be true of each part.
- **Example:** "This university is prestigious, so every department must be excellent."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the property in question transfer from whole to parts?

### No True Scotsman
- **Common names:** Moving the goalposts (definitional variant), ad hoc rescue, purity test
- **Definition:** Protecting a generalisation from counter-examples by retroactively redefining the group to exclude the counter-example.
- **Example:** "No responsible parent would let their child do X." (Counter-example: "But the Smiths are responsible parents and they do X.") "Well, no *truly* responsible parent would."
- **Diagnostic:** Has the category been redefined to exclude the counter-example, rather than the generalisation being revised?

### Special Pleading
- **Common names:** Double standard, exemption without justification
- **Definition:** Applying standards or rules to others but claiming an exemption for oneself (or one's position) without adequate justification.
- **Example:** "I know the deadline is firm for everyone, but my situation is different."
- **Diagnostic:** Is there a genuine, relevant difference that justifies the exemption, or is the exception merely self-serving?

## 3. Fallacies of Ambiguity

These fallacies exploit unclear or shifting language.

### Equivocation
- **Common names:** Double meaning, bait and switch (linguistic), wordplay fallacy
- **Definition:** Using a word or phrase in two different senses within the same argument, making the logic appear valid.
- **Example:** "The sign said 'fine for parking here,' so I parked there. It's fine!" ("Fine" shifts from "penalty" to "acceptable.")
- **Diagnostic:** Does a key term carry the same meaning each time it is used in the argument?

### Amphiboly
- **Common names:** Structural ambiguity, grammatical ambiguity
- **Definition:** The argument's conclusion depends on a grammatically ambiguous statement that can be read in more than one way.
- **Example:** "Visiting relatives can be boring." (Who is visiting? Who is boring?)
- **Diagnostic:** Could the statement's grammar support a different interpretation that changes the argument?

### Accent (Fallacy of Emphasis)
- **Common names:** Misleading emphasis, selective quoting, quoting out of context
- **Definition:** Changing the meaning of a statement by emphasising different words, or by extracting a passage from its original context.
- **Example:** A review says "This would be a great film *if* the plot made any sense." The advertisement quotes: "A great film!" — *The Review*
- **Diagnostic:** Has the original emphasis, qualifications, or context been altered?

### Persuasive Definition
- **Common names:** Stipulative redefinition, loaded definition
- **Definition:** Defining a term in a biased way that smuggles in the conclusion, then using that definition as a premise.
- **Example:** Defining "taxation" as "government theft" and then arguing that taxation is immoral because theft is immoral.
- **Diagnostic:** Does the definition embed the conclusion? Would a neutral definition change the argument?

### Motte-and-Bailey
- **Common names:** Strategic equivocation, retreat to the obvious
- **Definition:** Conflating two positions — one bold and controversial (the "bailey") and one modest and easily defended (the "motte"). When challenged on the bold claim, the arguer retreats to the modest one, then advances the bold one again once pressure subsides.
- **Example:** Bold claim: "All dietary science is corrupt." When challenged: "I just mean we should be careful about nutrition studies." Then later returns to dismissing all dietary science.
- **Diagnostic:** Is the arguer switching between a strong claim and a weak, defensible version of it?

## 4. Formal Fallacies

These are errors in the logical form of the argument. The conclusion does not follow from the premises by the rules of deductive logic.

### Affirming the Consequent
- **Common names:** Reverse conditional error, converse error
- **Definition:** From "If P, then Q" and "Q," concluding "P." The conditional only guarantees Q when P is true — Q can be true for other reasons.
- **Example:** "If it rains, the streets are wet. The streets are wet. Therefore, it rained." (A fire hydrant may have burst.)
- **Diagnostic:** Is the argument inferring the antecedent from the consequent?

### Denying the Antecedent
- **Common names:** Inverse error, fallacy of the inverse
- **Definition:** From "If P, then Q" and "not P," concluding "not Q." The conditional only tells us what happens when P is true — it says nothing about what happens when P is false.
- **Example:** "If you study, you'll pass. You didn't study. Therefore, you won't pass." (You might pass for other reasons.)
- **Diagnostic:** Is the argument inferring the negation of the consequent from the negation of the antecedent?

### Undistributed Middle
- **Common names:** Faulty syllogism, middle term error
- **Definition:** In a categorical syllogism, the middle term (the term connecting the two premises) is never distributed (never refers to all members of the category it names).
- **Example:** "All dogs are animals. All cats are animals. Therefore, all dogs are cats."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the middle term refer to all members of its category in at least one premise?

### Illicit Major / Illicit Minor
- **Common names:** Overextended conclusion, going beyond the premises
- **Definition:** A term that is distributed (refers to all members) in the conclusion but undistributed (refers to only some members) in its corresponding premise.
- **Example:** "All roses are flowers. No daisies are roses. Therefore, no daisies are flowers." (Major term "flowers" is distributed in conclusion but not in premise.)
- **Diagnostic:** Does any term refer to "all" members in the conclusion when it only referred to "some" in the premise?

### Fallacy of Four Terms (Quaternio Terminorum)
- **Common names:** Four-term fallacy, ambiguous middle
- **Definition:** A syllogism that appears to have three terms but actually has four because one term is used in two different senses. This is equivocation applied to syllogistic form.
- **Example:** "Nothing is better than eternal happiness. A ham sandwich is better than nothing. Therefore, a ham sandwich is better than eternal happiness." ("Nothing" shifts meaning.)
- **Diagnostic:** Does every term carry exactly the same meaning each time it appears?

### Non Sequitur
- **Common names:** "It does not follow," random conclusion, logical disconnect
- **Definition:** The conclusion does not logically follow from the premises. This is the broadest category of formal error — technically all formal fallacies are non sequiturs, but the label is used when no more specific fallacy applies.
- **Example:** "She drives an expensive car, so she must be a good manager."
- **Diagnostic:** Is there any valid logical path from the premises to this conclusion?

## 5. Causal and Inductive Fallacies

These fallacies involve errors in reasoning about evidence, causes, patterns, and generalisations.

### Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
- **Common names:** "After, therefore because," false cause (temporal), coincidence-as-cause
- **Definition:** Assuming that because event B followed event A, A caused B. Temporal sequence alone does not establish causation.
- **Example:** "I wore my lucky socks and we won the game. The socks caused the victory."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the causal claim based on anything more than temporal sequence?

### Cum Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
- **Common names:** Correlation-causation fallacy, "correlation is not causation"
- **Definition:** Assuming that because two things occur together, one causes the other. They may both be caused by a third factor, or the correlation may be coincidental.
- **Example:** "Ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer, so ice cream causes drowning." (Both are caused by hot weather.)
- **Diagnostic:** Has a causal mechanism been established? Have confounding variables been considered?

### Hasty Generalisation
- **Common names:** Jumping to conclusions, sweeping generalisation, insufficient sample, anecdotal evidence
- **Definition:** Drawing a broad conclusion from too few instances, an unrepresentative sample, or a single vivid case.
- **Example:** "I met two rude people from that city, so everyone there must be rude."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the sample large enough and representative enough to support the generalisation?

### Faulty Analogy (Weak Analogy)
- **Common names:** Bad comparison, false equivalence, comparing apples to oranges
- **Definition:** Drawing a conclusion based on a comparison between two things that are not sufficiently similar in the relevant respects.
- **Example:** "Running a country is just like running a business." (Countries have citizens with rights, not customers; they cannot go bankrupt and liquidate; they have territorial sovereignty; etc.)
- **Diagnostic:** Are the two things being compared similar in ways that are relevant to the conclusion? Are there important dissimilarities?

### Cherry Picking (Suppressed Evidence)
- **Common names:** Card stacking, selective evidence, one-sided argument, confirmation bias (in argumentation)
- **Definition:** Presenting only the evidence that supports the conclusion while ignoring or concealing evidence that contradicts it.
- **Example:** Citing three studies that support a claim while ignoring ten studies that contradict it.
- **Diagnostic:** Has the arguer considered all readily available evidence, or only the favourable portion?

### Survivorship Bias
- **Common names:** Survivor fallacy, overlooking failures
- **Definition:** Drawing conclusions only from cases that "survived" some selection process while ignoring the (often much larger) set of failures.
- **Example:** "These successful entrepreneurs all dropped out of college, so dropping out helps you succeed." (Ignores the vastly larger number of dropouts who did not become successful.)
- **Diagnostic:** Are the cases being examined representative, or only the visible survivors of a larger population?

### Gambler's Fallacy
- **Common names:** Monte Carlo fallacy, hot hand fallacy (inverse)
- **Definition:** Believing that past independent events affect the probability of future independent events.
- **Example:** "The coin has landed heads five times in a row, so it's due to land tails."
- **Diagnostic:** Are the events genuinely independent? If so, past outcomes have no bearing on future probabilities.

### Texas Sharpshooter Fallacy
- **Common names:** Clustering illusion, pattern-seeking fallacy
- **Definition:** Finding a pattern in data after the fact and treating it as if it were predicted in advance. Named after a marksman who shoots at a barn wall and then draws a target around the tightest cluster of bullet holes.
- **Example:** After a stock market crash, identifying the "warning signs" that were supposedly obvious beforehand.
- **Diagnostic:** Was the pattern identified before or after examining the data? Could the pattern be coincidental?

### Regression Fallacy
- **Common names:** Regression to the mean fallacy
- **Definition:** Failing to account for natural statistical regression toward the mean, and attributing the regression to a supposed cause.
- **Example:** "My headache went away after I took the homeopathic remedy." (Headaches naturally subside — regression to the mean.)
- **Diagnostic:** Could the observed change be explained by natural regression toward the mean rather than the proposed cause?

### Ecological Fallacy
- **Common names:** Group-to-individual error, aggregate fallacy
- **Definition:** Assuming that what is true of a group's aggregate statistics applies to each individual member.
- **Example:** "Country X has a high average income, so John, who lives in Country X, must be wealthy."
- **Diagnostic:** Is a group-level statistic being applied to an individual without justification?

## 6. Rhetorical and Procedural Fallacies

These are argumentative moves that, while not always errors in strict logic, undermine fair and honest discourse.

### Burden of Proof Shifting
- **Common names:** Proving a negative, "prove me wrong," reversal of onus
- **Definition:** Shifting the responsibility of proof to the person questioning a claim, rather than the person making it. The burden of proof lies with the one asserting a positive claim.
- **Example:** "Alien abductions are real. Prove they're not."
- **Diagnostic:** Who made the initial positive claim? Are they providing evidence, or demanding that others disprove them?

### Moving the Goalposts
- **Common names:** Raising the bar, impossible standard, shifting criteria
- **Definition:** Changing the criteria for proof or acceptance after the original criteria have been met.
- **Example:** "Show me evidence of X." (Evidence provided.) "Well, that's not *enough* evidence — show me Y instead."
- **Diagnostic:** Have the criteria for what counts as adequate evidence or proof changed during the discussion?

### Gish Gallop
- **Common names:** Proof by verbosity, shotgun argumentation, argument flooding
- **Definition:** Overwhelming an opponent with a rapid succession of many arguments, regardless of their accuracy or strength, making it impossible to address them all.
- **Example:** In a debate, listing 20 unrelated objections in 60 seconds, each requiring substantial time to refute.
- **Diagnostic:** Is the arguer relying on volume rather than quality? Are individual points sufficiently developed to evaluate?

### Tone Policing
- **Common names:** "Calm down," manner over matter, civility deflection
- **Definition:** Dismissing an argument by criticising the way it is expressed rather than engaging with its content.
- **Example:** "I can't take your argument seriously when you're so emotional about it."
- **Diagnostic:** Is the criticism directed at the argument's substance or only at its delivery?

### Sealioning
- **Common names:** Persistent "just asking questions," concern trolling, bad-faith engagement
- **Definition:** Making repeated, seemingly polite requests for evidence or explanation on a topic already well-established, with no genuine intent to engage with the answers.
- **Example:** "But can you really *define* what fairness means? What evidence do you have that fairness matters? Can you cite a study?" (Asked of an obvious moral principle, indefinitely.)
- **Diagnostic:** Are the questions genuine attempts to understand, or are they designed to exhaust and derail?

### Whataboutism (Red Herring variant)
- **Common names:** Tu quoque (extended), "what about...," deflection
- **Definition:** Responding to a criticism by pointing to a different issue, typically the critic's own failings or a different problem entirely, rather than addressing the original point.
- **Example:** "Our government's human rights record is criticised, but what about *their* human rights record?"
- **Diagnostic:** Does the response address the original claim, or does it deflect to an unrelated issue?

### False Balance (Argument to Moderation)
- **Common names:** Bothsidesism, golden mean fallacy, Argumentum ad Temperantiam
- **Definition:** Treating two positions as equally valid when the evidence strongly favours one, or assuming the truth lies exactly between two extremes.
- **Example:** "Some scientists say the earth is 4.5 billion years old; some say 6,000. The truth is probably somewhere in the middle."
- **Diagnostic:** Does the evidence actually support a position between the two, or is one position much better supported than the other?

### Kettle Logic
- **Common names:** Inconsistent defence, contradictory arguments
- **Definition:** Using multiple arguments to defend a position when those arguments are mutually inconsistent. Each argument may be individually coherent, but they cannot all be true simultaneously.
- **Example:** "I never borrowed your kettle. Besides, it was already broken when I borrowed it. And I returned it in perfect condition."
- **Diagnostic:** Are the multiple lines of defence logically compatible with one another?

### Poisoning the Well
- **Common names:** Preemptive ad hominem, prejudicial framing
- **Definition:** Presenting negative information about someone *before* they make their argument, to discredit them in advance so that anything they say is viewed with suspicion.
- **Example:** "Before Professor Singh presents her findings, you should know she was passed over for tenure."
- **Diagnostic:** Is background information being provided to genuinely inform, or to preemptively undermine credibility?
