This skill guides the reading, writing, and critique of persuasive texts. It provides specific, practical technique drawn from the strongest traditions in argumentation theory and prose style.

The user may be doing one of three things:

1. **Writing** — drafting or revising a persuasive text (essay, op-ed, brief, proposal, letter, speech)
2. **Reading/Critiquing** — analysing someone else's argument for strengths, weaknesses, fallacies, and cogency
3. **Learning** — asking about argument concepts, fallacies, reasoning forms, or rhetorical technique

Adapt your guidance to the task. For writing, be constructive and generative. For critique, be rigorous and specific. For learning, be clear and concrete with examples.

## Part I: Argument Structure and Cogency

A persuasive text is built on arguments. An argument is a set of claims (premises) offered as reasons to accept another claim (the conclusion). Everything starts here.

### Identifying Arguments

Before evaluating or improving a text, identify its arguments:

1. **Find the main conclusion.** What is the text ultimately trying to get the reader to accept or do? Look for conclusion indicators: *therefore, thus, hence, so, consequently, it follows that, this shows that, we can conclude.*
2. **Find the premises.** What reasons does the author offer? Look for premise indicators: *because, since, for, given that, as shown by, the reason is, assuming that.*
3. **Identify sub-arguments.** A premise in one argument may itself be the conclusion of another. Map the structure: which claims support which?
4. **Identify unstated premises.** Many arguments rely on assumptions the author doesn't make explicit. Surface them — they are often where weakness hides.
5. **Identify unstated conclusions.** Sometimes the author implies a conclusion without stating it. Make it explicit for evaluation.

### The ARG Conditions (Govier)

Govier's framework evaluates arguments on three independent conditions. A cogent argument satisfies all three. This is the backbone of argument evaluation:

**A — Acceptability of Premises**
Are the premises rationally worthy of belief? A premise is acceptable if:
- It is a matter of common knowledge or uncontroversial fact
- It is supported by testimony from a credible, unconflicted source
- It is established by a cogent sub-argument
- It follows from reliable observation or established theory
- It is a reasonable definitional or conceptual claim

A premise is *unacceptable* if:
- It is a known falsehood or contradicts well-established evidence
- It is an unsupported, controversial claim (smuggled assumption)
- It is vague or ambiguous to the point of being unfalsifiable
- It begs the question (presupposes what the argument is trying to prove)
- It is self-contradictory or contradicts other premises in the same argument

**R — Relevance of Premises to Conclusion**
Do the premises, if true, actually bear on the conclusion? A premise is relevant if its truth would increase the rational credibility of the conclusion. Relevance failures include:
- Premises that address a different question than the conclusion answers
- Emotional appeals substituting for evidence (*irrelevant appeal to pity, fear, force*)
- Attacks on the arguer rather than the argument (*ad hominem* used as evidence)
- Appeals to popularity, tradition, or authority where those aren't evidentially relevant

**G — Good Grounds (Sufficiency)**
Do the premises, taken together, provide enough support for the conclusion? Even if each premise is acceptable and relevant, the argument fails if:
- The evidence is too thin (hasty generalisation from too few cases)
- Obvious counter-evidence or counter-arguments are ignored
- The conclusion is far stronger than the premises warrant (overgeneralisation)
- The premises support the conclusion only in conjunction with further claims that are missing
- Alternative explanations are not considered or eliminated

When critiquing, evaluate each condition independently and report findings separately. When writing, verify each condition holds for every argument you advance.

### Forms of Reasoning

Persuasive texts deploy different forms of reasoning. Knowing which form is in play helps you evaluate whether the reasoning is sound:

**Deductive Reasoning**
The conclusion follows necessarily from the premises. If the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. Evaluate for *validity* (does the conclusion logically follow?) and *soundness* (are the premises also true?).
- Modus ponens: If P then Q; P; therefore Q.
- Modus tollens: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
- Disjunctive syllogism: P or Q; not P; therefore Q.
- Hypothetical syllogism: If P then Q; if Q then R; therefore if P then R.
- Categorical syllogisms: All A are B; all B are C; therefore all A are C.

**Inductive Reasoning**
The conclusion is made probable (but not certain) by the premises. Evaluate for *strength* (how probable does the evidence make the conclusion?) and *cogency* (is the argument strong with acceptable premises?).
- Generalisation: From observed instances to a general claim. Evaluate sample size, representativeness, counter-instances.
- Statistical syllogism: From a general statistical claim to a particular case.
- Causal inference: From observed correlation or controlled evidence to a causal claim. Watch for post hoc ergo propter hoc, confounding variables, reverse causation.
- Predictive reasoning: From past patterns to future expectations.

**Analogical Reasoning**
The conclusion is supported by comparison with a relevantly similar case. Evaluate by asking: Are the compared cases similar in ways that are *relevant* to the conclusion? Are there *relevant dissimilarities* that undermine the comparison? How many relevant similarities vs. dissimilarities exist?

**Abductive Reasoning (Inference to Best Explanation)**
The conclusion is the best available explanation of the observed evidence. Evaluate by asking: Does the explanation account for all the evidence? Is it simpler than alternatives (parsimony)? Is it consistent with background knowledge? Are there competing explanations that account for the evidence equally well or better?

**Conductive Reasoning (Govier)**
A distinctive form where multiple independent factors are weighed for and against a conclusion, without any single factor being decisive. Common in practical, ethical, and policy reasoning. Evaluate by asking: Have all relevant pro and con factors been identified? Have they been weighed fairly? Has the arguer acknowledged the factors that count against their conclusion?

**Presumptive Reasoning (Walton)**
Arguments based on defeasible presumptions — reasonable to accept unless defeated by counter-evidence. Walton's argumentation schemes (argument from expert opinion, argument from analogy, argument from consequences, etc.) each come with *critical questions* that, if unanswered, defeat the presumption. When you encounter such an argument, apply the relevant critical questions.

## Part II: Fallacy Identification and Elimination

Fallacies are errors in reasoning that undermine cogency. They can be subtle and often appear persuasive on the surface. The skill includes a comprehensive **Fallacy Library** — read `references/fallacy-library.md` for the full catalogue with technical names, common names, definitions, examples, and diagnostic questions.

### How to Use the Fallacy Library

When **critiquing** a text:
1. Read the argument carefully and identify its structure (premises → conclusion)
2. For each inferential step, ask: Does this step commit a known fallacy?
3. Consult the Fallacy Library by category (Relevance, Presumption, Ambiguity, Formal, Causal, Inductive) to systematically check
4. Name the fallacy by both its technical and common name
5. Explain *why* it is fallacious in this specific context — don't just label it
6. Offer a way the argument could be repaired or reframed to avoid the fallacy

When **writing**:
1. After drafting an argument, review each inferential step against the Fallacy Library
2. Pay special attention to the fallacies most common in your genre (policy writing is prone to false dilemma and slippery slope; opinion pieces to strawman and ad hominem; scientific writing to hasty generalisation and appeal to authority)
3. Stress-test by imagining a hostile but fair critic — what fallacies would they identify?
4. Pre-empt the most likely objections (see Counter-Arguments below)

### The Most Dangerous Fallacies (high frequency, high deceptiveness)

These deserve special vigilance because they are common and often pass unnoticed:

| Fallacy | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Strawman** | Feels like engagement with the opposition but isn't — distorts and defeats a weaker version of the real argument |
| **False Dilemma** | Narrows choice artificially — makes a preferred option seem inevitable when alternatives exist |
| **Begging the Question** | Circular: the conclusion hides in the premises, so the argument proves nothing |
| **Equivocation** | A key term shifts meaning mid-argument, making the logic appear valid when it isn't |
| **Ad Hominem (Abusive)** | Attacking the person feels relevant but doesn't address their argument |
| **Appeal to Ignorance** | Absence of evidence treated as evidence of absence (or vice versa) |
| **Hasty Generalisation** | A vivid example or small sample is taken as proof of a general claim |
| **Slippery Slope** | A chain of increasingly dire consequences is asserted without evidence for each link |
| **Red Herring** | A tangentially related but irrelevant point diverts attention from the real issue |
| **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc** | Temporal sequence confused with causation — "after, therefore because" |

## Part III: Counter-Argument and Refutation

Strong persuasive writing engages with opposition. Weak persuasive writing ignores it.

### Building Counter-Arguments (for critique)

When critiquing a text, construct counter-arguments by:
1. **Challenging premises directly.** Find the weakest or most controversial premise and argue that it is false or unsupported.
2. **Presenting counter-evidence.** Offer facts, data, or examples that contradict the argument's claims or conclusion.
3. **Proposing alternative explanations.** Show that the same evidence supports a different conclusion.
4. **Identifying unacknowledged trade-offs.** Show consequences or costs the author ignores.
5. **Applying the argument's logic to an absurd case.** If the reasoning generalises, show where it leads (reductio ad absurdum).
6. **Exposing hidden assumptions.** Surface unstated premises and show they are questionable.

### Integrating Counter-Arguments (for writing)

When writing, strengthen your argument by:
1. **Concession and rebuttal.** Acknowledge the strongest version of the opposing view, then explain why your position is stronger. ("While it is true that X, this does not undermine Y because...")
2. **Pre-emptive refutation.** Anticipate objections and address them before the reader raises them. This builds trust and demonstrates intellectual honesty.
3. **Steel-manning.** Present the opposing view in its strongest form — not a weakened caricature. Then defeat the strong version. This is the opposite of straw-manning and is the hallmark of rigorous persuasion.
4. **Qualifying claims.** Don't overclaim. "The evidence suggests" is often more persuasive than "This proves beyond doubt" because the reader trusts a writer who acknowledges limits.
5. **Addressing the strongest objection explicitly.** Identify the single most damaging counter-argument and devote space to rebutting it.

## Part IV: Rhetorical Strategy

Cogent argument is necessary but not sufficient for effective persuasion. You also need rhetorical strategy — the art of presenting your argument so that it actually reaches and moves the audience.

### The Rhetorical Appeals (Aristotle)

**Ethos (Credibility)**
The audience must trust the speaker. Build ethos by:
- Demonstrating knowledge and expertise (cite credible sources, show command of the subject)
- Showing fairness (acknowledge complexity, concede valid opposing points)
- Using appropriate register and tone (match the audience's expectations)
- Establishing common ground with the audience early

**Pathos (Emotion)**
Emotional engagement is legitimate when it supplements (not replaces) logical argument. Use pathos by:
- Concrete, specific examples rather than abstractions (a named individual's story vs. statistics alone)
- Vivid, sensory language that makes abstract consequences feel real
- Narrative structure that creates stakes and urgency
- Appeals to shared values, not just shared facts

**Logos (Reason)**
The logical backbone. Ensure your arguments are valid, well-evidenced, and well-structured. The techniques in Parts I–III constitute logos.

**Kairos (Timing and Context)**
The right argument at the right moment. Consider:
- What is the audience already thinking and feeling about this issue?
- What has just happened that makes this argument timely?
- What framing will resonate given the current context?

### Arrangement: The Architecture of Persuasion

The order in which you present arguments matters:

**Classical Arrangement (adapted from Cicero)**
1. **Introduction (Exordium):** Engage the reader. Establish the topic, its importance, and your credibility. Create goodwill.
2. **Background (Narratio):** Provide necessary context, facts, and framing. Present the situation fairly.
3. **Thesis (Propositio):** State your main claim clearly and explicitly.
4. **Proof (Confirmatio):** Present your arguments with evidence, reasoning, and examples. Strongest arguments first or last (primacy/recency effects) — not buried in the middle.
5. **Refutation (Refutatio):** Address counter-arguments. Concede where appropriate, rebut where necessary.
6. **Conclusion (Peroratio):** Summarise, reinforce the main claim, and end with impact — a call to action, a return to a powerful image, or an appeal to consequence.

This structure is not rigid — adapt it to genre and purpose. But the principle holds: *introduction → context → claim → evidence → counter-argument → conclusion* is the natural architecture of persuasion.

## Part V: Style for Persuasive Clarity

Persuasive writing fails when the reader can't follow the argument or is alienated by the prose. Style serves cogency.

### Core Principles (Strunk & White + Orwell)

**Omit needless words.** (Strunk & White, Rule 13)
Every word in a persuasive text should serve the argument. Vigorous writing is concise. This does not mean short — it means that every word does work.

> "A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts." — Strunk & White

**Use the active voice.** (Strunk & White, Rule 11; Orwell, Rule 4)
Active voice is more direct, vigorous, and honest. Passive voice obscures agency — who did what to whom. In persuasive writing, accountability matters. "The committee rejected the proposal" is stronger than "The proposal was rejected." Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.

**Put statements in positive form.** (Strunk & White, Rule 12)
Say what things *are*, not what they aren't. "He was dishonest" is sharper than "He was not honest." Negative constructions make the reader work harder and dilute force.

**Use definite, specific, concrete language.** (Strunk & White, Rule 14)
Abstract language is the enemy of persuasion. "The policy caused significant economic harm" is weaker than "The policy destroyed 14,000 jobs in the manufacturing sector." Specificity is credibility.

**Never use a long word where a short one will do.** (Orwell, Rule 2)
"Use" not "utilise." "End" not "terminate." "Help" not "facilitate." Pretentious diction signals insecurity, not authority. Clarity signals confidence.

**Never use a metaphor, simile, or figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.** (Orwell, Rule 1)
Dead metaphors ("level playing field," "at the end of the day," "tip of the iceberg") signal unoriginal thinking. If you must use a figure, make it fresh and precise.

**If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.** (Orwell, Rule 3)
Redundancy weakens. "In my personal opinion" → "I believe." "At this point in time" → "Now." "Due to the fact that" → "Because."

**Never use a foreign phrase, scientific word, or jargon where an everyday English equivalent exists.** (Orwell, Rule 5)
Jargon excludes the reader and often masks fuzzy thinking. Technical terms are acceptable only when they add genuine precision that plain language cannot achieve — and when the audience will understand them.

**Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.** (Orwell, Rule 6)
Rules serve clarity. When following a rule produces awkward, unclear, or dishonest prose, break it. This final rule is the most important because it subordinates all others to the overriding goal: clear, honest communication.

### Sentence-Level Technique

**Place the emphatic words at the end.** (Strunk & White, Rule 18)
The end of a sentence carries the most weight. Put your most important word or phrase there.
- Weak: "The economy will collapse, experts say."
- Strong: "Experts say the economy will collapse."

**Express coordinate ideas in similar form.** (Strunk & White, Rule 15)
Parallel structure creates rhythm and clarity. It signals that ideas of equal importance are receiving equal weight.
- Weak: "The plan is expensive, difficult to implement, and nobody wants it."
- Strong: "The plan is expensive, impractical, and unpopular."

**Keep related words together.** (Strunk & White, Rule 16)
Don't separate subject from verb, or verb from object, with long parenthetical insertions. The reader should not have to hold a sentence in memory to parse it.

**Vary sentence length deliberately.** 
A string of long sentences dulls the reader. A string of short ones feels choppy. Alternate. Use a short sentence after a long one for emphasis. The short sentence lands like a punch. Used well, sentence-length variation controls the reader's pace and attention.

### Paragraph-Level Technique

**Make the paragraph the unit of composition.** (Strunk & White, Rule 9)
One paragraph, one idea (or one step in the argument). Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence that signals what the paragraph will establish. The paragraph is the argumentative building block — each one should advance the argument by one step.

**Begin each paragraph with a sentence that signals its relationship to the last.**
Transitions are not decoration — they are the connective tissue of argument. The reader needs to know: Is this a new point? A supporting detail? A counter-argument? A consequence? Make it explicit.

**End paragraphs with impact.**
Like sentences, paragraphs carry weight at their close. The last sentence of a paragraph often provides the "so what" — the takeaway or transition that propels the reader forward.

## Part VI: Workflow Checklists

### Checklist: Critiquing a Persuasive Text

1. **Map the argument structure.** Identify the main conclusion, premises, sub-arguments, and unstated assumptions.
2. **Evaluate Acceptability.** Are the premises true, well-supported, or at least rationally acceptable? Flag any that are dubious, unsupported, or question-begging.
3. **Evaluate Relevance.** Does each premise actually bear on the conclusion? Flag red herrings, irrelevant appeals, and ad hominem attacks.
4. **Evaluate Sufficiency.** Do the premises, taken together, provide enough grounds? Is counter-evidence ignored? Is the conclusion stronger than the evidence warrants?
5. **Identify fallacies.** Consult the Fallacy Library (`references/fallacy-library.md`). Name each fallacy found, explain why it applies, and assess how severely it damages the argument.
6. **Construct counter-arguments.** Identify the strongest objection(s) to the argument. Present them fairly.
7. **Assess rhetorical strategy.** How does the author use ethos, pathos, logos? Are emotional appeals legitimate or manipulative? Is the arrangement effective?
8. **Assess style.** Is the prose clear, specific, and vigorous? Or vague, jargon-laden, and passive? Does the style serve or undermine the argument?
9. **Deliver a verdict.** Synthesise: Is the argument cogent? Partially cogent? What are its greatest strengths and most critical weaknesses?

### Checklist: Writing a Persuasive Text

1. **Clarify your thesis.** State your main claim in one sentence. If you can't, your argument isn't clear yet.
2. **Identify your audience.** What do they already believe? What will they resist? What evidence and framing will reach them?
3. **Build your argument structure.** Map premises → conclusion. For each premise, ask: Is it acceptable? Is it relevant? Do my premises together provide sufficient grounds?
4. **Stress-test for fallacies.** Review each inferential step against the Fallacy Library. Eliminate any fallacious reasoning.
5. **Steel-man the opposition.** Identify the strongest counter-arguments. Address them through concession and rebuttal.
6. **Arrange for persuasive impact.** Use classical arrangement or adapt it. Put strong arguments first or last. Address counter-arguments explicitly.
7. **Write with stylistic rigour.** Active voice. Concrete language. No needless words. Parallel structure. Emphatic endings.
8. **Revise with the ARG conditions.** One pass for Acceptability. One pass for Relevance. One pass for Sufficiency. Then a final pass for style and flow.
