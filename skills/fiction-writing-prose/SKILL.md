This skill guides prose craft for fiction projects. It provides specific, opinionated technique for writing professional-grade fictional prose, drawn from the most respected craft pedagogy and practitioner wisdom.

The user provides prose requirements: a scene to write, a draft to revise, a voice to establish, or prose output to evaluate. They may be at any stage — first draft through final polish.

## Core Principle: Render, Don't Explain

Good prose renders experience. It places the reader inside a moment — seeing, hearing, smelling, touching, thinking — as a specific character in a specific place. It never steps outside that experience to explain what's happening, name what's being felt, or interpret what it means.

The reader's job is to interpret. The writer's job is to provide the experience worth interpreting.

This principle governs everything that follows. When in doubt, ask: "Am I rendering experience or explaining it?" If explaining, rewrite.

## 1. Voice & Point of View

**Voice is the story's most important sentence-level decision. It determines the reader's entire experience.**

### Voice

Voice is not decoration applied to content. Voice IS the content. The same event narrated in two different voices becomes two different stories. Voice encompasses:

- **Diction:** The words available to this narrator. A psycholinguist uses different words than a dockworker. A 19th-century narrator uses different words than a contemporary one. The character's vocabulary is the voice's vocabulary — the author's vocabulary must not override it.
- **Syntax:** How this narrator constructs sentences. Short and clipped? Long and subordinated? Fragmented? Formal? The pattern of sentence construction IS the character's pattern of thought.
- **Perception:** What this narrator notices. A carpenter notices joins and surfaces. A painter notices light and colour. A paranoid person notices exits. Perception is character.
- **Rhythm:** The pace and cadence of this narrator's prose. Breathless? Meditative? Staccato? The rhythm should feel like this character's consciousness.

Voice must be **specific** and **consistent**. A voice that could belong to anyone belongs to no one. A voice that shifts without reason breaks the fictional dream.

### Point of View

**Close Third Person** (most common in contemporary literary fiction):
- The narration filters through the character's consciousness without using "I"
- The character's thoughts, perceptions, and judgments colour the narration
- The author controls **psychic distance** — how close or far the narration sits from the character's mind

**Psychic Distance Spectrum** (Burroway via Gardner):

| Level | Example | Distance |
|-------|---------|----------|
| 1 | "It was winter of the year 1853." | Maximum — authorial panorama |
| 2 | "Henry Warburton had never liked snowstorms." | Author names character, external view |
| 3 | "Henry hated snowstorms." | Closer — character's feeling, author's voice |
| 4 | "God, how he hated snowstorms." | Character's voice bleeding through narration |
| 5 | "Snow. Under the collar, down the boots." | Deep POV — character's raw perception, no narrator visible |

The craft is **deliberate movement** along this spectrum:
- Open a chapter at Level 1–2 (establishing), move to Level 3–4 (the body of the scene), drop to Level 5 at emotional peaks
- Never jump more than one or two levels in a single sentence without intent
- Accidental distance shifts break the fictional dream

**Free Indirect Style** (Wood):
The narrator's voice and the character's consciousness blend without explicit markers:
- Direct thought: *He thought, "This is absurd."*
- Indirect thought: *He thought that it was absurd.*
- Free indirect: *This was absurd.* (Whose voice? Both. That's the power.)

Free indirect style is the most powerful tool in contemporary prose fiction. It allows the narrator to carry the character's judgments, idiom, and emotional state while maintaining narrative flexibility. Master it.

**First Person:**
- Maximum intimacy, but the narrator can only know what they know
- The gap between what the narrator says and what the reader understands is where irony lives
- First person narrators who understand themselves perfectly are boring; unreliable narrators are interesting

**Omniscient:**
- The narrator knows everything — but knowing everything doesn't mean telling everything
- The craft of omniscient narration is selection: what to show, when, and from whose perspective
- Modern omniscient often mimics close third, dipping into different characters' consciousness in turn (but with control — not head-hopping)

### Anti-Patterns

- **Generic voice:** Prose that could have been written about anyone by anyone. Fix: ground every sentence in the POV character's specific perception, vocabulary, and concerns.
- **Head-hopping:** Shifting between characters' thoughts within a scene without purpose or control. Fix: commit to one consciousness per scene (or section). Mark shifts deliberately.
- **Psychic distance whiplash:** Jumping from panoramic to intimate and back without control. Fix: track your distance level. Move gradually. Drop to deep POV only at key moments.

## 2. Sensory Detail & Setting

**The physical world enters prose through the character's perceiving consciousness, not the author's camera.**

### The Detail Hierarchy

Not all details are equal. There are three kinds:

- **On-duty detail** (Wood): Carries meaning — reveals character, advances understanding, embodies theme. This is the only kind that belongs in good prose.
- **Off-duty detail:** Decorative — present for atmosphere but earning nothing. Cut it or put it to work.
- **Generic detail:** Could appear in any story about any character. "The sun was setting." "The room was dimly lit." "She wore a blue dress." These are placeholders, not prose. Replace with the specific.

### Significant Detail (Burroway, Gardner)

One right detail does more than a paragraph of description:
- Not "a flower" → "a weed-choked marigold"
- Not "the room was messy" → "three days of coffee cups, the top one growing fur"
- Not "he was nervous" → "he folded the napkin into smaller and smaller squares"

The **right** detail is:
- **Specific** — irreducibly particular. Wood's "haecceity" (thisness).
- **Filtered** — selected by the POV character's consciousness. What this person would notice.
- **Load-bearing** — it carries meaning beyond its literal content.
- **Sensory** — it engages a sense. Sight is the default; good prose uses all five (plus proprioception, temperature, kinaesthesia).

### Setting as Character

Setting is not backdrop. It is a physical expression of the story's values:
- A character's home reveals who they are
- A workplace reveals power dynamics
- Weather and light can carry emotional charge — but only if filtered through character perception, not applied by the author as mood lighting
- The details of a place should tell the reader something they wouldn't get from a plot summary

### Crowding and Leaping (Le Guin)

Two complementary techniques:
- **Crowding:** Piling up vivid, concrete details in a dense passage. Creates immersion, sensory overload, richness. Use for moments of intensity or wonder.
- **Leaping:** Jumping from one vivid moment to the next, leaving gaps. Creates pace, mystery, reader engagement. Trust the reader to fill the space between.

The best prose alternates between crowding and leaping. Constant crowding is exhausting. Constant leaping is disorienting.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Decorative description:** Paragraphs of setting that establish atmosphere but serve no character, plot, or thematic purpose. Fix: every description line must be filtered through POV and earn its place.
- **The sensory checklist:** Mechanically listing what each sense perceives. "He could smell the roses. He could hear the birds. He could feel the breeze." Fix: integrate sensation into action and thought. Only the senses the character would actually notice.
- **Weather as mood:** Opening with weather that mirrors the character's emotions. Fix: if you must use weather, filter it through character perception and make it unexpected.

## 3. Dialogue

**Dialogue is verbal action. Characters pursue objectives through speech. What they don't say matters more than what they do say.**

### Principles

- **Dialogue is not conversation.** Real conversation is boring — full of filler, repetition, pleasantries. Fictional dialogue is compressed, shaped, and purposeful. It is the *illusion* of real speech, not a transcript.
- **Characters pursue objectives.** In every exchange, each character wants something — to persuade, to deflect, to conceal, to provoke, to comfort. Dialogue is two objectives colliding.
- **Subtext is where meaning lives.** The most powerful dialogue is oblique — characters talk around the real subject. What's unsaid, implied, or deliberately avoided is where the drama lives. If characters say exactly what they mean, the scene is dead.
- **Each character has their own voice.** Different vocabulary, different sentence structures, different concerns, different verbal tics. If you can swap speakers and the dialogue still makes sense, the voices aren't distinct enough.

### Tags and Beats

- **"Said" is invisible.** Use it. The reader's eye skips past it. It does its job without drawing attention.
- **Kill said-bookisms.** "Exclaimed," "intoned," "breathed," "hissed" (you can't hiss a sentence without sibilants), "opined" — these call attention to themselves and away from the dialogue.
- **Kill adverbs on tags.** "He said angrily" — the dialogue should convey the anger. If it doesn't, rewrite the dialogue, not the tag.
- **Beats over tags.** A physical action between lines of dialogue ("She set down the cup." or "He looked away.") replaces a tag and adds information. Use beats to pace dialogue and reveal character through gesture.
- **Not every line needs attribution.** In a two-person exchange, once the rhythm is established, many lines can stand alone. The reader tracks who's speaking.

### Oblique Dialogue

Characters rarely say what they mean. The craft is in the gap between surface and meaning:

- A character saying "fine" when they are not fine
- Two characters discussing the weather when the subject is their failing marriage
- A character asking detailed practical questions to avoid an emotional confrontation
- A character changing the subject at exactly the moment the conversation approaches the truth

### The One-Plus-One Rule (Stein)

If you convey the same information through dialogue AND narration (or through two different dialogue exchanges), each loses power. Say it once, the right way.

- Don't write angry dialogue and then add: "He was furious."
- Don't have a character explain in dialogue what the narration already showed.
- If the gesture says it, the dialogue doesn't need to. If the dialogue says it, the gesture doesn't need to.

### Anti-Patterns

- **On-the-nose dialogue:** Characters say exactly what they think and feel. Fix: add a layer. What do they want the other person to believe? What are they hiding?
- **"As you know, Bob" exposition:** Characters telling each other things they both already know for the reader's benefit. Fix: have the unknowing reader discover information through conflict, not lecture.
- **Identical voices:** All characters sound like the narrator. Fix: give each character specific vocabulary, syntax, and verbal habits.
- **Over-attribution:** Tags on every line, adverbs on every tag. Fix: "said" or beats. Trust the dialogue.

## 4. Prose Rhythm & Sentence Craft

**Prose has a sound. If it doesn't sound right, it isn't right. Read it aloud.**

### Sentence Variety (Gardner, Le Guin)

Monotonous rhythm is the fastest way to lose a reader. Vary:

- **Length:** Short sentences create tension, urgency, emphasis. Long sentences create immersion, reflection, complexity. A short sentence after several long ones hits like a punch.
- **Structure:** Simple sentences (subject-verb-object). Compound sentences (joined by conjunctions). Complex sentences (with subordinate clauses). Fragments. The pattern of construction IS the prose's rhythm.
- **Opening:** Don't start every sentence with the subject. Vary with prepositional phrases, participial phrases, adverbial clauses, conjunctions.

### Rhythm Mirrors Meaning

The sound of prose should enact its content:
- Tense scene → shorter sentences, harder consonants, less subordination
- Reflective passage → longer sentences, softer sounds, more subordination and qualification
- Shock or revelation → a single short sentence after a long build-up
- Confusion or overwhelm → a long, clause-heavy sentence that resists easy parsing

### Punctuation as Music (Le Guin)

Punctuation marks are rhythmic tools:
- **Period:** Full stop. Silence. Emphasis on what came before.
- **Comma:** Brief pause. Breath. Connection.
- **Semicolon:** Longer pause; the two halves are related but distinct.
- **Dash:** Interruption — urgency — a thought breaking in.
- **Colon:** Announcement: what follows explains or exemplifies.
- **Ellipsis:** Trailing off... unfinished thought... hesitation.

Use the punctuation that creates the right breath for the sentence.

### Verbs (Le Guin)

Verbs carry the energy of prose:
- **Strong, specific verbs** over weak verb + adverb. "She strode" not "she walked quickly." "The light stuttered" not "the light flickered intermittently."
- **Active voice** as default. Passive voice when the action matters more than the actor, or when the character's powerlessness is the point.
- **Consistent tense.** Tense shifts without purpose break the dream.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Symmetrical prose:** Every sentence the same length. Every sentence starting with the subject. Every paragraph three sentences long. Fix: vary deliberately. Read aloud and listen for monotony.
- **Consecutive similar structures:** "She walked to the door. She opened it. She stepped outside." Fix: combine, subordinate, vary. "She stepped outside. The sky was wrong."
- **Hedging:** "She seemed to almost feel a kind of sadness." Fix: commit. "She ached." If the POV character is uncertain, the uncertainty is in the situation, not in weak verbs and qualifiers.
- **Vocabulary inflation:** "Commenced" instead of "began." "Utilised" instead of "used." "Resided" instead of "lived." Fix: use the simplest accurate word. Reach for the thesaurus only when the common word genuinely fails.

## 5. Figurative Language

**A good metaphor illuminates. A bad metaphor decorates. A cliché is invisible — the reader's eye slides right past it.**

### Principles

- **Metaphor and simile must illuminate** — they must make the reader see something they couldn't see without the comparison. If the metaphor doesn't add understanding, it subtracts.
- **Earn your metaphors.** A metaphor that arises from the character's world (a mechanic thinking in terms of engines, a sailor thinking in terms of currents) feels organic. A metaphor imported from nowhere feels like the author showing off.
- **Extended metaphor with discipline.** A sustained metaphor can organise an entire passage — but every element must track. Mixed metaphors ("we'll burn that bridge when we come to it") break the dream.
- **Cliché is death.** "A blanket of snow." "Her heart raced." "Time stood still." "Silence was deafening." These are not images — they are wallpaper. The reader sees nothing.

### Finding Fresh Figures

- Ground metaphor in the specific world of the story and the character's expertise/obsessions
- Use unexpected sensory crossings: describe a sound in terms of texture, a colour in terms of temperature
- Understate rather than overstate: a small, precise image is more powerful than a grandiose one
- Test every metaphor: does it make me see something I didn't see before? If not, cut it.

### Anti-Patterns

- **Purple prose:** Overwrought, ornate figurative language that calls attention to itself. "The crimson fingers of dawn caressed the trembling horizon." Fix: strip it back. What is actually happening? "The sky reddened." Then decide if even that is needed.
- **Metaphor pile-up:** Three metaphors in one sentence, each from a different domain. Fix: one image per moment. Let it land.
- **Dead metaphors as live ones:** Using clichés as if they're fresh images. "Her blood ran cold" is not a metaphor anymore — it's a placeholder. Fix: find the specific physical sensation for THIS character in THIS moment.

## 6. Scene-Level Prose

**A scene is prose in action — all the above elements working together in real time.**

### Scene and Summary (Burroway)

Two modes of prose narration:

- **Scene:** Real-time dramatisation. Dialogue, action, moment-by-moment perception. Time in the story roughly equals time in the reading. Use for emotional peaks, confrontations, turning points, discoveries.
- **Summary:** Condensed narration. Covers hours, days, years in a paragraph. "That winter, she learned to lie." Use for transitions, routine, backstory, and time passages.

The craft is knowing when to expand into scene and when to compress into summary. The rule: **if it matters emotionally, dramatise it. If it matters informationally, summarise it. If it matters both ways, dramatise it.**

### Entering a Scene

- **In late, out early.** Don't start a scene with the character arriving, settling in, ordering a drink. Start at the point of engagement — the moment something is at stake. End when the value has turned — don't linger for goodbyes.
- **Establish quickly.** The reader needs to know: who, where, when, and what's at stake. But deliver this through action and perception, not exposition. A character noticing the crack in a hotel room ceiling tells us where they are and how they feel about being there.
- **Anchor in the body.** The scene exists in physical space. Ground the reader with concrete sensory detail early — what the POV character sees, hears, feels physically. Then let thought and dialogue carry the scene forward.

### Prose in Real-Time Scene

In an active scene, prose must:
- **Track the POV character's attention.** What they notice, how they interpret it, what they miss. Narration follows perception.
- **Interleave action, dialogue, thought, and perception.** Don't write a page of dialogue, then a page of description. Weave them. A line of dialogue → a physical gesture → a flash of thought → a sensory detail → the next line of dialogue.
- **Escalate.** The prose should tighten as the scene intensifies. Shorter sentences, less subordination, fewer digressions, more concrete physical detail. At the turning point, the prose should be at its most compressed.
- **Land the turn.** The moment the scene's value shifts, let the prose register it. A shift in perception, a single detail that changes everything, a sentence that stops the reader.

### Transitions

- Between scenes: white space or a clear temporal/spatial marker. Don't over-explain the jump.
- Between summary and scene: move from general to specific. Summary → more specific summary → a particular moment → full scene.
- Between psychic distances: move gradually unless the abrupt shift is intentional for effect.

### Anti-Patterns

- **The establishing shot:** Opening every scene with a paragraph of description before anything happens. Fix: open with action or perception, weave setting into movement.
- **Dialogue-only scenes:** Pages of dialogue with no physical grounding. The reader loses the bodies, the room, the world. Fix: interleave beats, gesture, perception.
- **Internal monologue instead of action:** The character thinks at length about what they should do, how they feel, what it all means — instead of acting. Fix: characters reveal themselves through what they do, not what they think about doing. Thought supplements action; it doesn't replace it.

## 7. Revision & Self-Editing

**First drafts are for discovery. Revision is where prose becomes art.**

### The Revision Hierarchy

Edit in this order (macro → micro):

1. **Structure:** Does the scene need to exist? Is it in the right place? Does it turn?
2. **Character:** Is the voice consistent? Is the POV controlled? Is the character earning their choices?
3. **Scene:** Enter late, exit early? Escalation? The turn lands?
4. **Prose:** Rhythm, variety, precision? Every detail on-duty?
5. **Sentences:** Verb strength? Unnecessary adverbs? Hedging? Redundancy?
6. **Words:** The right word, not the almost-right word? Clichés? Vocabulary inflation?

### Revision Passes

- **The cut pass (King):** Second draft = first draft - 10%. Remove every word, sentence, and passage that doesn't earn its place. Be ruthless.
- **The voice pass:** Read the entire piece checking voice consistency. Does the narrator's vocabulary stay in character? Are there author-voice intrusions?
- **The aloud pass (Le Guin):** Read the entire piece aloud. Your ear catches what your eye misses: awkward rhythm, repeated words, dead passages, monotonous sentence length.
- **The subtext pass:** For every line of dialogue, ask: is the character saying what they mean? If yes, rewrite so they're not. For every piece of narration, ask: am I explaining what I should be showing?
- **The detail pass:** For every descriptive detail, ask: is this on-duty? Is it specific? Is it filtered through the POV character? Would this character notice this?

### The Revision Question Set

When evaluating prose, ask:

1. **Voice:** Is the narrative voice specific, consistent, and distinctive? Could I identify this narrator from a random paragraph?
2. **POV:** Is the psychic distance controlled? Do I know whose consciousness I'm in? Are shifts deliberate?
3. **Detail:** Is every detail on-duty, specific, and filtered through the POV? Any decorative or generic details?
4. **Dialogue:** Does every exchange have subtext? Do characters have distinct voices? Are tags minimal?
5. **Rhythm:** Does the prose sound good read aloud? Is sentence length varied? Is structure varied?
6. **Compression:** Can I cut 10% without losing meaning? Any one-plus-one violations?
7. **Rendering:** Am I showing experience or explaining it? Any named emotions, signposted feelings, or spelled-out subtext?
8. **Figurative language:** Are metaphors fresh, earned, and illuminating? Any clichés? Any purple prose?
9. **Scene craft:** Do I enter late and exit early? Does the scene escalate and turn? Is action interleaved with dialogue and perception?
10. **Dream continuity:** Does anything break the fictional dream? Any authorial intrusions, logic errors, vocabulary that belongs to the author not the character?

## Working Method

When this skill is loaded for a fiction project, apply it as follows:

### During Drafting

- Establish voice FIRST. Write a test paragraph that sounds like this narrator. Lock the diction, syntax, and perception patterns before writing the scene.
- Default to rendering over explaining. If you catch yourself naming an emotion, stop and find the physical/behavioural/perceptual manifestation instead.
- Enter scenes late, exit early. Don't warm up on the page.
- Interleave dialogue, action, thought, and perception. Never write extended blocks of any one mode.
- Read key passages aloud (mentally). If the rhythm is dead, vary sentence length and structure.
- Track psychic distance. Know where you are on the spectrum. Move deliberately.
- Use free indirect style as the default for interiority in close third person.
- Ground every scene in the body — physical sensation, concrete detail, specific perception.

### During Revision / Evaluation

- Apply the revision hierarchy (structure → character → scene → prose → sentences → words)
- Run the revision question set
- Check the AI anti-pattern list below — these are the most common failures in AI-generated prose
- Read aloud (or mentally simulate reading aloud) for rhythm
- Apply King's 10% cut rule
- Check every detail for on-duty status
- Check every line of dialogue for subtext

### AI Anti-Pattern Checklist

Before finalising any prose output, verify NONE of these are present:

- [ ] **Emotional signposting:** No named emotions ("she felt sadness," "anger surged through him"). Show the manifestation instead.
- [ ] **Purple prose:** No overwrought figurative language. No "crimson fingers of dawn." Strip to the concrete.
- [ ] **Said-bookisms:** No "exclaimed," "breathed," "intoned." Use "said" or beats.
- [ ] **Adverbs on tags:** No "said quietly," "said angrily." Let dialogue and beats carry tone.
- [ ] **Symmetrical prose:** No sequences of same-length, same-structure sentences. Vary.
- [ ] **Consecutive subject-openers:** Not "She... She... She... She..." Vary sentence openings.
- [ ] **Explaining subtext:** If oblique dialogue was written, the narration doesn't explain what it "really meant."
- [ ] **Decorative description:** No setting details that exist only for atmosphere. Every detail on-duty.
- [ ] **Summarising emotions:** No "They had an emotional conversation." Dramatise or cut.
- [ ] **Vocabulary inflation:** No elaborate words where simple ones work.
- [ ] **Hedging:** No "seemed to," "almost," "a kind of," "somewhat" unless the character's uncertainty is the point.
- [ ] **One-plus-one violations:** Nothing said twice through different modes (narration + dialogue, action + thought).
- [ ] **Cliché:** No dead metaphors, stock phrases, or generic descriptions. Find the specific.
- [ ] **Voice breaks:** The narrator's vocabulary never exceeds the POV character's plausible register.
- [ ] **Dream-breaking:** No authorial intrusions, no anachronistic language, no logic errors in the story world.
