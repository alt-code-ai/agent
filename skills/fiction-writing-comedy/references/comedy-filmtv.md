# Comedy in Film & Television
## Reference: Sitcoms, Comedy Films, Screenwriting, and the Writers Room

> Load this reference when helping with comedy screenwriting — sitcom scripts, comedy films, sketch writing, or any screen comedy. The SKILL.md covers universal mechanics; this file covers screen-specific execution.

---

## Sitcom Structure: The Architecture of 22 Minutes

The 22-minute sitcom is the most constrained and formally developed comedy form. Every element serves multiple functions simultaneously.

### A/B/C Plot Architecture
- **A-story**: The protagonist's main conflict, carries emotional weight
- **B-story**: Comic subplot, usually supporting characters, provides tonal contrast and breathing room
- **C-story / runner**: A minimal thread — a recurring gag that pays off in the tag scene

The three tracks create rhythm: when the A-story gets heavy, cut to B; when B is broad, cut to C. The audience never needs recovery time if you manage the intercutting.

### The Cold Open
Self-contained comic premise before the credits. Functions as a hook and a tonal promise. Seinfeld's diner conversations establish register. The Office's cold open pranks demonstrate character and relationship. The cold open earns the audience's attention before the episode asks for commitment.

### Act Breaks in Comedy
Different from drama. Don't end on a cliffhanger — end on a **reversal or complication**: the character's plan backfires, or new information reframes everything. The act break must be funny while also being a pressure-ratchet.

### Economy
22 minutes allows no setup-only scenes. Every scene must advance plot *and* generate at least one laugh. If a scene is pure exposition with no comedy, it's a scene you haven't found yet.

## Steve Kaplan: The Hidden Tools of Comedy

Kaplan's framework is the most useful single resource for structuring screen comedy.

### The Non-Hero
The comic protagonist lacks the skills, authority, or awareness their situation demands — but proceeds anyway. Michael Scott believes he's beloved; Larry David believes social contracts are enforceable; Basil Fawlty believes he deserves better than he gets. None of them are heroes in any conventional sense. Their fundamental inadequacy is what generates comedy.

**Why heroes don't work in comedy**: A competent, capable protagonist solving problems isn't comic — it's satisfying. Comedy requires a gap between what the situation demands and what the character can provide.

### Hope vs. Expectation
The character hopes things will work out. The audience, who can see both sides, expects them not to. Kaplan calls this gap the comedy engine. It requires the audience to be simultaneously inside the character's hope and outside it — seeing the gap the character can't see.

### The Comic Worldview
Every strong comic character has a specific, distorted lens through which they misread everything. This is different from being stupid — it's being intelligently wrong in a consistent way. Michael Scott's comic worldview: he genuinely believes performance is reality (if he acts like a great boss, he is one). Larry David's: he genuinely believes social rules are logical and enforceable. These worldviews generate scenes because they clash with a reality that works differently.

### "Fun" vs. "Funny"
Kaplan distinguishes: fun is pleasurable (a witty character being clever). Funny requires vulnerability and struggle. The character in genuine trouble, who doesn't fully grasp their trouble, is funny. The character who's just charming and clever is fun. You need funny.

## Tina Fey and the Writers Room

*Bossypants* translates improv rules into writing principles.

### Yes-And
Accept the premise your collaborator offers and build on it. In the writers room: don't shut down pitches, extend them. Even a bad pitch contains something — the yes-and reveals what it contains. **The writers room is not the place to evaluate; it's the place to generate.**

### Specificity as Comedy
Don't bring a "gun" to a scene — bring "a bedazzled Derringer your aunt left you." Specific details are inherently funnier because they imply character and world. Generic details require the audience to imagine; specific details do the work for them.

**Rule**: If you can replace your detail with a more specific version, do it. Keep replacing until you can't get more specific.

### Commitment
Playing the absurd premise completely straight. The moment a character winks at the audience — breaks the fourth wall to acknowledge the absurdity — the comedy deflates. The absurd must be played with total seriousness. The gap between the gravity of the performance and the absurdity of the situation is where the laugh lives.

## Farce: Architectural Comedy

Farce is the most structural comedy form — it's built like clockwork, and when it works it feels like inevitability.

### The Door
Characters who must not meet are perpetually almost meeting. The door is the literal mechanism: behind one door is the person who'd blow everything up. The whole scene is built around that door not opening. When it finally opens, everything explodes.

### Escalation Rules
1. Each complication must generate *more* complications, never resolve them
2. The character's attempts to fix the situation always make it worse
3. The audience must be able to see the next disaster coming slightly before the character does
4. Resolution, when it comes, must be total — farce cannot be partially resolved

### The Ticking Clock
Pressurize everything with a deadline. The clock makes the audience's relationship to the escalation urgent, not just amusing.

### Mistaken Identity
The misunderstanding must be sustainable — the character who could reveal the truth must have a reason not to. Once the misunderstanding has a reason, the spiral can begin. Each scene, the web tightens.

## Cringe Comedy: The Naturalistic Register

Curb Your Enthusiasm and The Office (UK) created a form built on social awkwardness as the primary comic resource.

### The Protagonist Who Cannot Stop
Larry David and David Brent double down when any reasonable person would retreat. This is the comic character's defining feature at its most extreme — the inability to disengage from the situation that's destroying them. The audience occupies two positions: understanding the social norm being violated and being unable to prevent the violation.

### Why Naturalism Makes It Worse
The handheld camera, mumbled dialogue, awkward silences — these make the situation feel real. Real-feeling situations trigger the social anxiety that makes cringe comedy excruciating. The form works by making the audience feel like they're witnessing something that's actually happening.

### The Look to Camera
The Office's Jim-looks-at-camera is the cringe comedy's key device: breaking the fourth wall not to comment, but to register shared recognition. It makes the audience complicit. They're not watching — they're there.

## Single-Camera vs. Multi-Camera

**Multi-camera** (Friends, Seinfeld, Big Bang Theory): Performed for a live audience. Broader performances, longer dialogue scenes. The laugh track creates a call-and-response rhythm — the audience laughs, then the scene waits, then continues. The jokes must be hearable over the laugh.

**Single-camera** (The Office, 30 Rock, Atlanta): No live audience. Allows subtlety — reaction shots, visual comedy, awkward silence. The comedy can be in what's *not* said, in the pause that extends one beat too long, in the reaction shot rather than the action.

**The choice**: Multi-camera requires the comedy to be in the dialogue; single-camera allows it to be in the cut. Know which form you're in before you write. A multi-camera scene needs stronger joke text; a single-camera scene can underwrite the dialogue if the edit can carry the comedy.

## Writing Physical Comedy on the Page

### The Keaton Principle
The face that doesn't react to what's happening makes the gag funnier. Buster Keaton's deadpan during increasingly catastrophic situations works because the absence of reaction creates dissonance. Write physical comedy by specifying what the character *doesn't* do as much as what they do.

### Stage Directions for Physical Comedy
Don't write "he falls." Write what the camera sees, beat by beat. The specificity of the physical description is what allows a director/actor to execute it.

> "He steps on the rake. The handle rises. He watches it rise. He does not move. The handle hits him. He absorbs this. He picks up a second rake."

### The Sight Gag on the Page
Write what's visible, in the order it's visible. The surprise must be in the *last* visual element described. Everything before it sets the wrong expectation.

## Apatow: Emotional Truth as Comedy Foundation

Judd Apatow's comedy films (*The 40-Year-Old Virgin*, *Knocked Up*, *Funny People*) are built on a serious emotional core — loneliness, fear, grief — that makes the comedy land harder because the stakes are real.

**Principle**: The funnier you want the comedy to be, the more seriously you must take the drama. An audience that genuinely cares about a character will laugh harder at their discomfort than at a character they have no relationship with.

**Improvisation as discovery**: Apatow uses improvisation not as performance decoration but as a way to find the truthful joke through repetition. The scripted version establishes the beat; improvisation finds the *best* version of the beat.

## Writers Room: Collaboration Culture

**Pitch out loud**: Comedy pitches must be spoken. Written pitches can't convey timing or tone.

**Yes-and, not no-but**: The room's job is to generate, not evaluate. Evaluation happens in the room but after the generation. The writer who immediately explains why an idea won't work is the room's cancer.

**Finding the form**: A joke has found its form when the room laughs *and* it serves character. The laugh alone isn't enough — the joke must be *this character's* joke, arising from their specific worldview.

**Don't top other people's jokes**: If someone pitches a joke and you immediately pitch a better version, you've killed both jokes. First, yes-and theirs. Then yours can come out of the yes-and.

**The funniest person in the room**: Often can't structure a story. The best comedy writer can do both — or knows when to yield to the person who can do the other thing.
