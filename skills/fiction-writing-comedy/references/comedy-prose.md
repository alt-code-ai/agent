# Comedy in Prose Fiction
## Reference: Novels, Short Stories, and Humorous Essays

> Load this reference when helping with comedy in written prose — novel, short story, personal essay, comic memoir, or any written form. The SKILL.md covers universal mechanics; this file covers prose-specific execution.

---

## Wodehouse: The Model Craftsman

P.G. Wodehouse is the technical standard for prose comedy. Every technique here can be found, perfected, in the Jeeves canon.

### The Delayed Plant
The setup is buried as a throwaway detail, early enough to be forgotten. When the payoff arrives, it feels both surprising and inevitable. Gussie Fink-Nottle's newts are mentioned casually in chapter two; by chapter twelve, they're load-bearing plot architecture. **Principle**: Plant the setup so naturally it reads as world-building, not setup.

### The Extended Comic Simile
Wodehouse's signature: take the emotional state, then find an image from a completely unrelated world that maps onto it with surgical precision.

> "He looked like a man who, picking daisies on the railway track, has just caught the down express in the small of the back."

Construction: (1) identify the exact emotional state, (2) find an image from an alien domain that captures it, (3) extend the image just far enough to prove the mapping wasn't accidental. The simile **earns its length** by accumulating detail that keeps correctly mapping. A bad comic simile is merely random. A good one is *surprising but inevitable*.

### The Unreliable Comic Narrator
Bertie Wooster is catastrophically wrong about everything and reports with complete confidence. The gap between Bertie's interpretation and observable reality *is* the joke. The reader occupies two positions: inside Bertie's logic and outside it simultaneously.

### The Terrifying Aunt
Not just Wodehouse's device — a principle: the comedy engine is the character who represents social consequence. Aunt Agatha functions because she makes stakes feel real in a world where nothing truly bad happens. Every comic world needs its enforcer.

### Timing Through Structure
- Short sentence after a long setup. A paragraph of elaborate setup, then: "It didn't."
- The paragraph break is a beat. Use it. Land the punchline, then break before the next sentence begins.
- Sentence rhythm carries comic timing. The clause that arrives too early deflates. The one that arrives exactly when expected becomes the *least* funny version.

## Waugh: Black Comedy and the Flat Affect

Evelyn Waugh's method: horror reported with the emotional register of a train timetable.

In *Decline and Fall* and *A Handful of Dust*, terrible things happen and the narrator doesn't flinch. When little John Andrew is killed in a hunting accident, the narrative simply moves on. The comedy — if that's the word — emerges from the *absence* of expected emotional response.

**The understatement technique**: "He was in some ways unsatisfactory." This carries the weight of a complete psychological portrait through what it refuses to say. The reader supplies the horror.

**Principle**: Comedy at the darkest register requires the narrator's emotional detachment to be absolute. Any flicker of feeling — sympathy, horror, sadness — collapses the ironic distance and leaves you with neither comedy nor tragedy, but muddy middle ground.

## Pratchett: Second Voice, Deep Structure

### The Footnote
Pratchett's footnote is a second narrative voice that can digress, editorialize, and deliver the joke the main text is too dignified to make. In *Small Gods*, footnotes carry philosophical arguments the plot can't pause for. The effect: two comedic registers running simultaneously, each able to react to the other.

If you don't use literal footnotes, find the equivalent: the parenthetical aside, the narrator who speaks directly to the reader, the chapter-end note.

### Comic Worldbuilding Through Specificity
The Ankh-Morpork Assassins' Guild has a school with a curriculum. The City Watch has bureaucratic procedures. The comedy of specificity: the absurd premise rendered with complete internal consistency. Don't just say the universe is ridiculous — give it ridiculous paperwork.

### The Serious Novel Inside the Funny One
Every Discworld novel contains a genuine argument about power, belief, justice, or death. The comedy is the delivery mechanism. The medicine goes down because it's wrapped in a joke. **Principle**: The funniest writers have something to say. The jokes are how they say it without the audience realizing they're being told something.

## Sedaris: The Comic Persona

David Sedaris constructs a version of himself who is slightly more hapless than the original. The "David Sedaris" of the essays is a *character*, not a confession.

**Controlled self-deprecation**: He is never so pathetic that sympathy overwhelms laughter. He's embarrassed, not destroyed. The reader laughs *with* him because he's already laughing at himself.

**Specificity as truth**: Brand names, exact prices, precise phrases — "I'm a recovering alcoholic but I'm also an alcoholic, so I choose to focus on the recovering part." The specific detail authenticates the scene and does the comedy work simultaneously.

**Escalation pattern**: A mildly embarrassing situation becomes mortifying through the narrator's attempts to manage it. Each fix makes it worse. This is the fundamental comedy engine: the protagonist cannot stop.

## Scene Architecture for Prose Comedy

### The Escalation Arc
Every comic scene runs on escalation. Structure:
1. The situation has potential for embarrassment/chaos
2. The protagonist attempts to manage it
3. The attempt makes it worse
4. A new element compounds the problem
5. Maximum chaos — all threads collide simultaneously
6. Partial resolution (enough to end the scene; not enough to cancel the setup for the next)

The scene climaxes at the point where every strategy has failed. Then: one small movement toward order, and out.

### The Dignity Violation
The central scene engine: a character who needs to maintain status has status systematically destroyed. Works because it satisfies two drives simultaneously — superiority (we feel above them) and incongruity (a person behaving like a machine, unable to adapt).

Map the character's dignity against the scene's escalation. The funnier your scene is, the more specifically that dignity matters to the character.

### Wrong Register
A character who uses formal language in an informal situation (or vice versa) signals their disconnection from reality. The language tells us everything we need to know about the character's specific delusion.

## Character Voice in Comic Prose

**The gap character**: Doesn't understand what the reader understands. The gap between their interpretation and reality is the joke's permanent engine. Bertie Wooster, Ignatius Reilly, Don Quixote.

**The straight-man narrator**: Reports absurdity without editorializing. Nick Carraway mode. Trusts the reader to find what's funny — which they will, harder, because you didn't tell them.

**The committed absurdist**: Christopher Moore's narrators — they take the absurd world completely seriously. The moment the narrator winks at the reader ("isn't this ridiculous?"), you've lost them.

## What Kills Prose Comedy

- **The laboured metaphor**: You loved it. It shows. Cut it.
- **Telling us it's funny**: "The hilarious scene that followed..." — kills the joke before it arrives.
- **Stand-up transplants**: Jokes that require vocal delivery die on the page. Restructure them as scene, or cut.
- **Sentimental intrusion**: Breaking a comic scene to remind us the character has feelings undercuts both comedy and emotion. Comedy and sentiment can coexist — but one must never *apologize* for the other.
- **The explained simile**: "He looked like a fish out of water — by which I mean he was uncomfortable." The explanation is the murder weapon.
- **Too many jokes too close together**: Comedy needs breathing room. Let one land before pitching the next.

## The Rule of Three in Prose

Two items establish a pattern; the third breaks it. Applies at every level:

- **Sentence**: "He was intelligent, handsome, and on fire."
- **Paragraph**: Two paragraphs of careful build-up; the third delivers the wrong thing.
- **Scene**: Two attempts to fix the problem; the third makes it catastrophically worse.
- **Novel/story**: Two arcs running as expected; the third goes somewhere else entirely.

The rule is not a formula — it's a description of how expectation works. Two points define a line; the third can go anywhere. That anywhere is the joke.
