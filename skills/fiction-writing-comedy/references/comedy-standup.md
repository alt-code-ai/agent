# Comedy in Stand-Up
## Reference: Material Development, Structure, and Performance Writing

> Load this reference when helping with stand-up comedy — writing new material, developing premises, diagnosing why bits aren't working, or structuring a set. The SKILL.md covers universal mechanics; this file covers stand-up specifically.

---

## Greg Dean: The Structure of a Joke

Dean's *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy* gives the most rigorous mechanical account of how jokes work.

**The two stories**: Every joke contains two separate narratives. The setup establishes Story 1 — the world the audience assumes they're in. The punchline delivers Story 2 — a different, reinterpreted world. The audience's brain switches between them at the moment of laughter.

**The connector**: A word or phrase that bridges both stories. The setup leads the audience to assume one interpretation of the connector. The punchline reveals that the connector was pointing at something else the whole time. The laugh is the moment of reinterpretation — the brain's "click" as Story 2 suddenly makes sense.

**Example**: "I told my wife she was drawing her eyebrows too high. She looked surprised." — *surprised* is the connector: Story 1 (emotional surprise at criticism) → Story 2 (eyebrows positioned to create expression of surprise). The punchline reinterprets everything.

**Why this matters**: When a joke isn't landing, the connector is usually wrong — either too obvious (the audience gets to Story 2 too early) or too obscure (they can't find Story 2 at all). Diagnose by finding the connector and testing whether it can carry both stories.

## Jerry Seinfeld: Precision as the Craft

Seinfeld's documented process: observe the specific mundane detail others pass over, then find the *exact* word — not a synonym, the one precise word that captures the experience. He reportedly spent a decade on six minutes of refined material.

**The observational premise**: "What is the deal with X?" is a simplified form. The real structure: identify an experience everyone recognizes but has never articulated, then articulate it precisely enough that the recognition produces a laugh. The laugh is partly relief — *finally, someone said it*.

**Specificity as precision**: A Geo Metro is funnier than a car. The specific word works because it proves the observation is real, not generic. Generic observations feel like they could belong to anyone; specific ones feel true.

**Comedy as editing**: Seinfeld's late work demonstrates that comedy is subtractive. The bit starts long and rough; you remove everything that isn't necessary until what remains is the minimum the joke requires. This is the opposite of writing more — it's writing less until it's right.

## Steve Martin: Character Over Jokes

*Born Standing Up* documents a radical experiment: what if the comedian *is* the joke, rather than the jokes being the joke?

Martin's stage persona was committed to absurdism regardless of audience response. He removed punchlines. He told jokes that weren't jokes. He played banjo during a bit because the incongruity between the moment and banjo music *was the bit*. The audience laughed at the act of watching someone perform comedy so badly, with such total commitment, that it became something else.

**The principle**: The comedian as character means the performance itself is the content. The jokes work because of *who is telling them*, not what they say. This requires absolute commitment to the persona — any break in character and the construct collapses.

**Application**: When you're stuck on joke-level fixes, sometimes the solution is at the character level. Who is this comedian? What do they believe about the world? Every joke should be legible as coming from this specific person.

## Mike Birbiglia: Vulnerability as Structure

Birbiglia's stand-up (*Sleepwalk with Me*, *Thank God for Jokes*) is built on narrative structure with vulnerability as the structural engine, not the emotional climax.

**"The thing I wasn't supposed to tell you"**: The joke's power comes from the feeling that the teller is confessing something they should keep private. But the vulnerability isn't the payload — it's what drives the story forward.

**The arc**: A premise that seems manageable, escalation through a specific, embarrassing series of events, a moment of genuine self-revelation, a consequence. The comedy is in the specificity of the events and the narrator's inability to stop themselves.

**Distinction from confessional comedy**: The story uses personal material, but it's *shaped* material — selected, edited, ordered for maximum effect. It only feels like confession. It's actually craft disguised as confession.

## Act-Outs: The Body Sells the Bit

An act-out is when the comedian stops narrating and *becomes* a character in the story — physically embodying the scene. The audience stops imagining the scenario and sees it.

**Why it works**: Full physical commitment signals to the audience that the comedian believes in the world they're creating. Disbelief collapses the scene. Commitment sells it.

**Construction**: Narrate to the moment, then drop narration and act it. Return to narration after the scene. The transition is instantaneous — no "and then I said" before you do the voice.

**The Keaton principle applied to stand-up**: The comedian's face that *doesn't* react to what they're describing is often funnier than the one that does. Deadpan during the act-out amplifies because the audience's reaction fills the space the comedian refuses to fill.

## Premises vs. Jokes

This is the most important structural distinction in stand-up.

**A premise** is a true observation — an insight about how things actually work that the comedian has identified and can articulate. The premise is *what the bit is about*.

**Jokes** are instances of the premise. They demonstrate the premise from different angles, apply it to specific situations, build and extend it.

**Why the distinction matters**: Weak stand-up stacks disconnected jokes. It might get laughs, but there's nothing to remember, nothing that accumulates. Strong stand-up mines one premise deeply — the audience can feel the comedian has thought seriously about this. When the premise is genuinely true, the jokes feel inevitable rather than invented.

**Test**: Can you state in one sentence what the bit is really about? If not, you probably have jokes without a premise.

## Callbacks: Structural Payoff

A callback returns to an earlier setup with new context. The second laugh is larger than the first because:
1. The audience has partially forgotten it — so the return is surprising
2. The connection proves the comedian was planning this — the audience is rewarded for paying attention
3. The setup accumulates meaning across the whole set

**Construction**: Plant the callback early enough to be half-forgotten. The return must reframe the original in a way that wasn't predictable. The callback that merely repeats the first joke is not a callback — it's a running gag.

**Don't telegraph**: If the audience sees the callback coming, it falls flat. The setup should feel complete on its own.

## Hannah Gadsby: Deconstructing the Form

*Nanette* (2018) is the most significant formal intervention in stand-up comedy in decades. Gadsby explains, within the show, what she's doing: comedy requires releasing tension (the punchline), but sitting with trauma means *not* releasing it. So she builds tension — and refuses to resolve it.

**What this reveals**: Stand-up's form has a built-in ideological bias. It always resolves. Gadsby exposed this by refusing resolution and describing, explicitly, what that refusal means.

**Application for writers**: Understanding the form's default lets you make deliberate choices about it. You can follow the form, subvert it, or — if your material requires it — refuse it. But you have to know what you're doing.

## Failure Modes in Stand-Up Writing

**The too-long setup**: The audience solves the joke before you arrive. Cut until it's the minimum needed to establish the wrong expectation.

**Explaining after the miss**: If the joke doesn't land, explaining it makes it worse. Move on. The explanation is a second murder.

**The "you know what I mean?" hedge**: This is the comedian outsourcing the laugh — asking the audience to do the work of finding the funny rather than delivering it. It signals distrust of both the material and the audience.

**Generic observation**: If your premise could be anyone's premise — if every comedian could say it — it isn't a premise, it's a topic. Premises are specific enough to be *yours*.

**Punching down**: Targeting people with less power than the comedian makes the audience protective. The right direction to punch: upward (power, pretension, stupidity in authority), inward (yourself), or sideways (shared absurdities).

## Process: How Material Gets Made

**The notebook**: Capture raw observations before they're shaped. The unprocessed thought often contains the real premise — the craft comes in extracting and sharpening it.

**Road-testing**: No bit is finished until it's been performed. Written comedy and performed comedy are different things. Material that reads well can die; material that reads rough can kill.

**The failing bit is information**: When a bit doesn't work, it's telling you something. Usually: the connector isn't working, the setup is misplaced, or the premise isn't quite true. Don't throw out the bit — diagnose it.

**Killing darlings**: The joke you love most is often the one working against you. Your attachment to it is the tell. If the bit would be better without it, cut it.
