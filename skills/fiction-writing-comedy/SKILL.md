# Fiction Writing — Comedy

Comedy is a craft, not a talent. Everything that's funny was made that way through specific decisions about structure, timing, voice, and the precise management of expectation. This skill gives you the working principles behind all those decisions.

## The Foundations: Why Things Are Funny

Four theories, each capturing something real:

**Incongruity** (Kant/Schopenhauer): We laugh when expectation collapses into something else. The punchline replaces the expected world with a better, wronger one. Comedy lives in the gap between the map and the territory.

**Superiority** (Hobbes): Laughter is "sudden glory" — the flash of self-congratulation at perceiving someone else's misfortune or foolishness. Every pratfall is a tiny throne.

**Relief** (Freud): Jokes discharge accumulated psychic energy. Comedy lets us say what we're not supposed to say through socially acceptable channels. The joke is a pressure valve.

**Benign Violation** (McGraw): Something is funny when it's simultaneously a violation *and* benign. Too safe = boring. Too dangerous = just cruel. The comic zone is the knife-edge between them.

**Bergson's rule**: We laugh when a person acts like a machine — rigid where they should adapt. Comedy is "the mechanical encrusted on the living." The absent-minded professor walking into the lamppost is the model. The automaton must be mocked back into humanity.

**Vorhaus's formula**: Comedy = truth + pain. The comic character crashes against reality because they cannot see it clearly. The gap between who they believe they are and who they obviously are is the engine. Their comic *perspective* — the specific distorted lens through which they misread everything — generates all the jokes.

## Universal Mechanics (Apply to Every Form)

**Setup/punchline**: The setup builds a frame of expectation (Story 1). The punchline shatters it and replaces it with a better interpretation (Story 2). The connecting word or phrase is the hinge — it bridges both worlds. Make the audience walk firmly into the wrong one.

**Rule of three**: Two items establish a pattern. The third detonates it. "He had intelligence, charm, and a crippling fear of geese." The setup length earns the right to the break.

**Specificity**: A dog is less funny than a Pomeranian. A car is less funny than a 1994 Geo Metro. Vague is the enemy of funny. Specificity signals authority, creates imagery, and does the work of a whole paragraph.

**Callbacks**: Return to earlier material after it's been forgotten. The callback gains power from accumulated meaning — it proves the audience was paying attention, and rewards them for it. Set it up without telegraphing it.

**Tags**: After a punchline lands, extend it with secondary punchlines. Ride the wave while the wave is still moving.

**Misdirection**: Lead the audience's attention firmly down the wrong path, so the right one hits harder. The misdirection must be genuinely convincing — not a trick, but a better story than the real one.

**Dignity violation**: The moment a character's carefully maintained self-image collapses, publicly. One of comedy's most reliable engines across every form (Basil Fawlty's thirty years of unearned dignity; the Prom Queen covered in pig blood).

**Status comedy**: High-status figures brought low, low-status figures revealed as competent. Comedy is the great leveler. Status reversals work because they're both satisfying and secretly true.

**What kills comedy**: Explaining the joke. Telegraphing ("this is hilarious"). Punching down — targeting the powerless makes audiences protective, not amused. Sentimentality adjacent to comedy smothers it. The too-long setup bleeds away tension. Hedging ("I don't know if this is funny, but...") asks forgiveness before you've tried.

## The Comedy Writer's Diagnostic

When something isn't landing, run through these:

1. **Is the setup too long?** The audience solved it first. Cut until it's the minimum needed to establish the wrong expectation.
2. **Is the surprise actually surprising?** If they can see it coming, it's not misdirection — it's a slow-motion pratfall.
3. **Is it specific enough?** Replace every vague element with the specific version.
4. **Is there a victim?** Comedy requires a target. If nothing and no one is at risk, there's no tension to release.
5. **Are you punching down?** If the subject of the joke is less powerful than the person making it, the audience becomes protective.
6. **Is the comic register holding?** A sentimental intrusion in a comic scene doesn't add depth — it cancels the comedy and earns no feeling in return.
7. **Are you explaining?** The explanation *is* the killer. Write the joke. Trust the reader/audience. Stop there.
8. **Is there commitment?** The absurd must be played straight. The moment anyone winks, the spell breaks.

## Source Authorities

**Theory**: Bergson (*Laughter*, 1900); Freud (*Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious*); McGraw & Warner (*The Humor Code*); Vorhaus (*The Comic Toolbox*)

**Prose**: Wodehouse (model the delayed plant, the extended simile); Waugh (black comedy, flat affect); Pratchett (second voice, comic worldbuilding, satire delivery); Sedaris (controlled self-deprecation, specificity); Moore (earned absurdity, genre subversion)

**Stand-up**: Dean (*Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy* — premise/connector framework); Seinfeld (precision, editing as craft); Martin (*Born Standing Up* — character over jokes); Birbiglia (vulnerability as engine); Gadsby (*Nanette* — formal deconstruction)

**Film/TV**: Kaplan (*The Hidden Tools of Comedy* — non-hero, hope vs expectation); Fey (*Bossypants* — yes-and, specificity); Gervais/Merchant (cringe comedy, naturalism); Apatow (emotional truth as comedy foundation)
