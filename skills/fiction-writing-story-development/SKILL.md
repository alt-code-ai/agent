This skill guides narrative development for fiction projects. It provides structured frameworks for building stories from premise through scene-level construction, drawn from the most respected and widely-used story development methodologies across film, television, and prose fiction.

The user provides narrative requirements: a story concept, characters, a thematic concern, or an existing draft that needs structural work. They may be at any stage — from initial concept to structural revision of a complete draft.

## Core Principle: Story is Transformation Under Pressure

A story is not a sequence of events. A story is the progressive transformation of a character's situation through escalating conflict, driven by a premise that unifies every element. Events are the mechanism; transformation is the story.

Every decision in narrative development — structure, character, scene, dialogue, setting — must serve this transformation. If an element doesn't pressure the character toward change or reveal something essential about the story's argument, it weakens the narrative.

## 1. Premise & Controlling Idea

**Before structure, before character, before scene — establish what the story argues.**

A premise is a proposition the story proves through dramatic action. It is not a topic ("love," "power," "identity") but an argument with a character, a conflict, and an outcome:

- "Ruthless ambition leads to destruction" (Macbeth)
- "Love prevails when self-sacrifice overcomes fear of loss"
- "Comfortable complicity dissolves when perception deepens beyond the system's reach"

**The premise test:** If you cannot state your premise in one sentence containing a character disposition, a conflict, and a resolution direction — the story has no spine. Stop and find it before proceeding.

**Controlling idea (McKee):** The value-charged meaning of the story expressed through the climax — what has changed and why. The controlling idea is the premise proven by the story's ending.

**Moral premise (Schechter):** "[Virtue] leads to [positive outcome], while [vice] leads to [negative outcome]." This must be true for both protagonist and antagonist — they are opposite proofs of the same proposition.

**Designing principle (Truby):** The story's unique organising logic — not theme, but the deep structural pattern that generates the story's form. "Track a man's development from birth to death in one day" (Ulysses). Literary fiction often has a designing principle that replaces conventional plot mechanics.

### Technique

- State the premise before outlining. Revise it as the story develops, but never lose it.
- Test every major element against the premise: does this character, scene, or event advance, test, or complicate the argument?
- The premise should be arguable — a story that proves something obvious ("cruelty is bad") has no dramatic tension.
- For complex literary fiction, the premise may be a question rather than an assertion — but the story's climax must still constitute an answer (even an ambiguous one).

### Failure Modes

- **No premise:** The story meanders. Events happen but nothing accumulates. Fix: find the argument.
- **Premise as lecture:** The story stops to explain its theme. Fix: the premise is proven through action and consequence, never through exposition or character speeches.
- **Premise too simple:** The story feels thin or predictable. Fix: complicate the premise. Add irony — McKee's "negation of the negation" where the worst outcome wears the mask of the best.

## 2. Character Architecture

**Characters generate plot. Build them from need and wound outward, then put them under pressure.**

### Character as Origin of Story (Egri)

Know your character's:
- **Physiology:** Physical traits, appearance, health, abilities — what the world sees
- **Sociology:** Class, education, work, relationships, community position — where they sit in the social structure
- **Psychology:** Drives, fears, contradictions, moral disposition, intelligence, temperament — what moves them internally

If you know these three dimensions deeply, the character's actions under pressure become inevitable. Plot emerges from character, not the reverse.

### Need and Wound

Every protagonist needs two things (Truby):
- **Psychological need:** What they must learn about themselves (internal)
- **Moral need:** How they are hurting others or themselves, usually without knowing it (external)

The **wound** is the origin of the need — a formative experience that created the character's flawed way of operating. The wound explains why the character resists change.

### True Character vs. Characterisation (McKee)

- **Characterisation** is the observable surface: mannerisms, speech patterns, habits, appearance
- **True character** is revealed only under pressure — the choices made when something real is at stake
- **Dimension** comes from contradiction within plausible human nature: a generous person who is secretly envious, a brave person who is emotionally cowardly

### Transformation Arcs

Three fundamental arc types:

- **Positive arc:** Character begins with a flaw/wound, is forced through escalating pressure to confront it, and transforms — becoming more capable, more moral, or more whole.
- **Negative arc (tragedy/corruption):** Character begins with a flaw, doubles down under pressure, and is destroyed or diminished by it.
- **Flat arc (testing):** Character already knows the truth; the story tests whether they can maintain it under pressure. The character transforms the world rather than being transformed.

The arc is not a switch (flawed → healed). It is a progressive, fought-for, reversible process. The character resists change. Change is forced by consequence.

### Orchestration (Egri)

Characters must be orchestrated for maximum friction:
- If two characters agree on everything, one is redundant
- Each character should test a different aspect of the protagonist's weakness
- Supporting characters embody alternative responses to the story's central question

### The Opponent

The antagonist is the most important character after the protagonist (Schechter, McKee):
- The opponent is the hero of their own story
- The opponent pursues the same goal or a directly competing one
- The opponent embodies the other side of the moral premise
- The strongest opponents are **right about something** — they have a legitimate perspective that the protagonist must reckon with
- A **web of opposition** (Truby) — multiple characters who each oppose the protagonist differently — creates richer conflict than a single antagonist

### Prose-Specific Notes

Prose has access to interiority that film does not. Use it:
- The reader can know what the character thinks, feels, remembers, and fears — but this access must be earned and controlled
- Interiority is most powerful when it contradicts exterior action (saying one thing, thinking another)
- Avoid using interiority as a substitute for dramatic action — thought without consequence is not story
- Character rendering in prose can evolve with the narrative (e.g., descriptions of another character becoming more complex as the POV character's perception deepens)

## 3. Dramatic Structure

**Structure is progressive complication — escalating conflict that forces harder and harder choices.**

### The Structural Hierarchy (McKee)

Story structure operates at nested levels:

- **Beat:** The smallest unit — an exchange of action and reaction that shifts a value. "She asks; he deflects" is a beat.
- **Scene:** One or more beats that turn a value from positive to negative (or vice versa). Every scene must turn. If a scene doesn't change something, it's not a scene.
- **Sequence:** 2–5 scenes building to a moderate reversal. A sequence has its own mini-arc.
- **Act:** A sequence of sequences building to a major reversal that changes the direction of the story. Act climaxes are the story's structural pillars.
- **Story:** The complete arc from inciting incident through progressive complications to climax and resolution.

### Structural Frameworks

Choose the framework that best fits the story's needs. These are not mutually exclusive — they describe the same underlying principles from different angles:

**Three-Act Structure (Field, McKee):**
- Act I (Setup/Thesis): Establish the ordinary world, the character's flaw, the inciting incident that disrupts equilibrium
- Act II (Confrontation/Antithesis): Progressive complications, rising stakes, escalating conflict. The longest act. Contains the midpoint reversal.
- Act III (Resolution/Synthesis): Crisis, climax, resolution. The character faces the ultimate test of their transformation.

**Hero Goal Sequence (Schechter):**
1. Hero has a flaw/wound
2. An opportunity arises
3. Hero resists the opportunity (reluctant hero)
4. Point of no return forces commitment
5. Pursuit through rising obstacles
6. Major setback/reversal
7. Hero must face their flaw directly
8. Climactic confrontation
9. Resolution — character transformed (or confirmed in tragedy)

**15-Beat Sheet (Snyder):**
Opening Image → Theme Stated → Set-Up → Catalyst → Debate → Break into Two → B Story → Fun and Games → Midpoint → Bad Guys Close In → All Is Lost → Dark Night of the Soul → Break into Three → Finale → Final Image

**Seven Key Steps (Truby):**
Weakness/Need → Desire → Opponent → Plan → Battle → Self-Revelation → New Equilibrium

**Hero's Journey (Vogler/Campbell):**
Ordinary World → Call → Refusal → Mentor → Threshold → Tests/Allies/Enemies → Inmost Cave → Ordeal → Reward → Road Back → Resurrection → Return

### Choosing a Framework

- For **commercial/genre fiction:** Snyder's beat sheet or Schechter's HGS give the most concrete structural guidance
- For **literary fiction:** Truby's seven steps or McKee's gap theory — they allow organic structure that grows from character
- For **short stories:** Compress to the essential arc — often just: equilibrium → disruption → escalating complication → crisis → epiphany/resolution. Charters' unity of effect is the governing principle.
- For **novellas:** Three-act structure with compressed Act II, or a sequence-based structure (4–6 sequences, each with its own arc)
- Use the Hero's Journey as a **diagnostic tool** ("where is my character in the journey?"), not a prescriptive template

### The Gap: The Engine of Drama (McKee)

The gap between expectation and result is the source of all dramatic tension:

1. Character assesses the situation and takes action expecting a result
2. The world reacts differently — a gap opens between expectation and result
3. The character must now take a more difficult action
4. Progressive complication: each gap is wider, stakes are higher, actions are more desperate

This principle operates at every structural level — within beats, scenes, sequences, and acts.

### Prose-Specific Notes

- Film structure is timed (page ≈ minute). Prose structure is proportional — beats map to narrative weight, not word count.
- Prose can sustain longer setup phases (Act I) because interiority and voice create engagement independent of plot momentum.
- Short stories often compress or subvert three-act structure — the epiphanic structure (Joyce) builds entirely toward a moment of revelation.
- Novels can sustain multiple structural frameworks simultaneously (A-plot in three acts, B-plot as a journey, thematic structure as a spiral).

## 4. Scene Construction

**Every scene must turn a value. Every scene must have conflict. Every scene must advance the story.**

### The Scene Obligation

A scene must accomplish at least one of:
- Turn a value (something changes from positive to negative or vice versa)
- Reveal character under pressure (a choice that exposes true character)
- Advance the plot (move the story toward the next complication or the climax)
- Deepen the theme (test or complicate the premise)

The best scenes accomplish all four simultaneously.

### Scene Architecture

1. **Desire:** What does the POV character want in this scene? (Scene-level goal, connected to story-level desire)
2. **Conflict:** What opposes that desire? (Another character, the environment, internal resistance)
3. **Turning point:** The moment the value shifts — the gap opens between what was expected and what happened
4. **Change:** The scene ends in a different value state than where it started

### Beat-Level Construction

Within a scene, beats are the micro-exchanges that build toward the turning point:
- Action → reaction → new action
- Each beat subtly shifts the power dynamic
- Beats build — they don't just alternate. The scene must escalate.
- The turning point is the beat where the scene pivots — the moment that changes everything that follows

### Scene-to-Scene Progression

Scenes must connect causally, not just chronologically:
- **"Therefore" and "but"** connectors (not "and then"): Scene A happens, *therefore* Scene B follows, *but* the result is unexpected, *therefore* Scene C...
- If you can replace "therefore" or "but" with "and then" between any two scenes, the connection is too weak

### Exposition in Scenes (McKee, Snyder)

- Never write a scene whose sole purpose is to deliver information
- Exposition is ammunition — load it into scenes that are already dramatically charged
- "Pope in the pool" (Snyder): deliver necessary information while something entertaining or tense is happening
- In prose: exposition can be woven into perception, action, and dialogue. Free indirect style is particularly effective — the character's voice carries information naturally.
- The reader should need the information before they receive it — create the question, then answer it

### Prose-Specific Notes

- Prose scenes can include what a camera cannot see: thought, memory, physical sensation, olfactory detail
- Scene transitions in prose can be more fluid than in film — a sentence can bridge hours, locations, or perspectives
- A prose scene's "value" can be internal (a shift in understanding) rather than external (a change in circumstances) — but there must still be a shift
- Scenes in prose can be very short (a single paragraph) or very long — length serves pacing, not convention

## 5. Compression & Economy

**What you leave out strengthens what remains.**

### The Iceberg Principle (Hemingway via Charters)

The dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water. The writer must know everything about the story world — history, context, backstory — but put only what is essential on the page. What is omitted strengthens the story if the writer knows what they are omitting.

### Multi-Function Elements

Every element in the story should serve multiple functions:
- A setting detail that also reveals character and advances theme
- A line of dialogue that also exposes conflict and delivers exposition
- A plot event that also tests the premise and pressures the character's wound

In short fiction, this is mandatory. In longer forms, it's the difference between tight and baggy prose.

### Cutting Discipline

Ask of every scene, character, detail, and line:
- Does this serve the premise?
- Does this advance the story (change a value, reveal character, complicate the situation)?
- If I remove this, does the story weaken — or does the reader not notice?

If the answer to the third question is "the reader doesn't notice," cut it.

## 6. Story Diagnostics & Revision

**When a story isn't working, diagnose before prescribing.**

### Common Story Problems and Their Structural Causes

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Story feels aimless | No clear premise / controlling idea | Find or sharpen the premise |
| Middle sags | Insufficient progressive complication | Widen the gaps — each obstacle must be harder than the last |
| Climax feels unearned | Character hasn't been tested enough | Add complications that specifically pressure the character's flaw |
| Ending feels arbitrary | Climax doesn't prove the premise | Realign the climax to deliver on the controlling idea |
| Character feels flat | No contradiction, no pressure | Add dimension (internal contradictions) and put them in situations that force hard choices |
| Antagonist feels weak | Opponent is merely an obstacle, not a person | Give the opponent their own legitimate goal and perspective |
| Story feels predictable | Gaps are too small — expectation matches result | Widen the gaps. Subvert expectation. Let the worst happen. |
| Story feels episodic | Scenes connect chronologically, not causally | Apply the "therefore/but" test to every scene transition |
| Exposition dumps | Information delivered outside of conflict | Restructure to deliver information under dramatic pressure |
| Theme feels heavy-handed | Premise stated through dialogue or narration | Remove all statements of theme. Prove it through action and consequence only. |
| Too many characters | Redundant functions / no orchestration | Merge characters who serve the same function. Ensure each tests a different aspect. |
| Stakes feel low | No genuine risk to the character | Ensure the character stands to lose something they cannot afford to lose |

### The Revision Question Set

When evaluating a story or story plan, ask:

1. **Premise:** Can I state it in one sentence? Does the climax prove it?
2. **Character:** Does the protagonist have a wound, a need, and a contradiction? Are they transformed (or confirmed) by the climax?
3. **Opposition:** Is the antagonist formidable, motivated, and the hero of their own story?
4. **Structure:** Does every scene turn a value? Does every act end with a major reversal?
5. **Gaps:** Does each complication open a wider gap between expectation and result?
6. **Causality:** Can I say "therefore" or "but" between every scene?
7. **Exposition:** Is every piece of information delivered under dramatic pressure?
8. **Compression:** Does every element serve multiple functions?
9. **Stakes:** What does the character stand to lose? Is it enough?
10. **Unity:** Does every element serve the premise?

## Working Method

When this skill is loaded for a fiction project, apply it as follows:

### During Planning / Story Development
- Establish the premise before outlining structure
- Build characters from Egri's triad (physiology, sociology, psychology) + wound/need
- Choose a structural framework appropriate to the form and genre
- Map the story's major turning points (act climaxes, midpoint, crisis, climax)
- Orchestrate characters for maximum friction
- Plan scenes with desire, conflict, turning point, and value change
- Apply the "therefore/but" test to the scene sequence
- Verify the climax proves the premise

### During Drafting
- Every scene must turn a value — check before moving to the next scene
- Exposition enters only under dramatic pressure
- Let the gap between expectation and result drive each scene forward
- Track the inner journey alongside the outer journey
- Ensure compression — every element serves multiple functions

### During Revision / Evaluation
- Use the diagnostic table to identify structural problems
- Apply the revision question set
- Check for scenes that don't turn, exposition that isn't dramatised, and gaps that are too narrow
- Verify the premise is proven through action, not stated through dialogue

### Adapting Film/TV Technique for Prose
- Replace visual storytelling with sensory detail and concrete imagery
- Use interiority (thought, memory, sensation) as a dramatic tool — but only when it contradicts or complicates exterior action
- Map beat sheet timing to narrative proportion, not word count
- Use free indirect style for exposition — the character's voice carries information naturally
- Short stories compress structure; novels can layer multiple structural frameworks
- The "fun and games" phase in prose is exploration/discovery — the character (and reader) inhabiting the new world
