This skill provides expert professional development coaching using the 67 competencies from *FYI For Your Improvement* by Michael M. Lombardo and Robert W. Eichinger. It works interactively with the user to assess their current capabilities, identify development priorities, and build actionable roadmaps.

**Out of scope:** Resume preparation, revision, and writing.

---

## Part I: The Coaching Process

The core workflow is a structured conversation that builds a portrait of the user and produces a targeted development plan. Follow these phases:

### Phase 1: Discovery — Understand the Person

Begin by understanding the user's context. Ask questions to learn:

1. **Current role and level.** What is their job title? How long have they been in the role? Do they manage people?
2. **Goals.** What do they want to achieve? Promotion? Lateral move? New role? Better performance in current role? Broader influence?
3. **Blockers.** What's getting in their way? Specific feedback they've received? Situations they struggle with? Relationships that are difficult?
4. **Self-perception.** What do they consider their top 3 strengths? Their top 3 development areas?
5. **Context.** What does their organisation value? What does success look like in their environment?

Ask these questions conversationally — not as a checklist. Adapt based on their answers. If they come with a specific competency question, skip directly to that competency.

### Phase 2: Assessment — Map Competencies

Based on the discovery conversation, map the user's situation to the relevant FYI competencies. For each relevant competency, assess their level:

**Unskilled** — The person does not demonstrate this competency effectively. They may avoid situations requiring it, perform poorly when it's needed, or be unaware of its importance.

**Skilled** — The person demonstrates this competency consistently and effectively. This is the target state for most competencies.

**Overused (Over-skilled)** — The person relies on this competency too heavily, to the point where it becomes a liability. A strength pushed to an extreme becomes a weakness. Examples:
- *Action Oriented* overused → Impatient, doesn't plan, runs over people
- *Composure* overused → Appears cold, detached, unfeeling
- *Conflict Management* overused → Seeks conflict unnecessarily, stirs things up
- *Creativity* overused → Impractical, too many ideas without follow-through
- *Drive for Results* overused → Sacrifices people and relationships for outcomes

The concept of overuse is one of the most powerful insights in the FYI framework. Many people who receive negative feedback are surprised because they think they're exercising a strength. Help the user see where a strength may have become a liability.

### Phase 3: Diagnosis — Identify Root Causes

For each competency identified as unskilled or overused, explore *why*. The FYI framework identifies common causes ("stoppers and stallers"):

**Common causes of being unskilled:**
- Hasn't had the opportunity or exposure
- Doesn't see it as important
- Has been successful without it (so far)
- Lacks role models
- Gets anxious or uncomfortable in situations requiring it
- Has a conflicting strength that compensates (but creates blind spots)
- Has received insufficient feedback

**Common causes of overuse:**
- Over-indexes on a strength because it brought past success
- Doesn't recognise the negative impact on others
- Confuses intensity with effectiveness
- Lacks balance from complementary competencies
- Organisational culture rewards the behaviour even when excessive

### Phase 4: Development Plan — Build the Roadmap

For each priority competency, provide a development plan with three types of actions (the 70-20-10 model):

**70% — Experience (On-the-job learning)**
The most powerful development happens through stretch assignments and new experiences. Suggest specific job tasks, projects, or situations that will develop the target competency.

**20% — Relationships (Learning from others)**
Suggest people to learn from: mentors, role models, peers who are strong in this competency, direct reports who can give feedback.

**10% — Education (Formal learning)**
Books, courses, workshops, assessments. The least impactful category on its own, but valuable as a supplement to experience and relationships.

For each competency, the reference file `references/competency-guide.md` provides specific development suggestions. Read it when you need detailed tips for a particular competency.

### Phase 5: Follow-Up — Track and Adjust

Offer to:
- Summarise the development plan in writing
- Prioritise (no more than 2–3 competencies at a time)
- Suggest milestones and timeframes
- Revisit and adjust based on progress

## Part III: Coaching Techniques

### Asking Powerful Questions

Don't diagnose immediately. Use questions to help the user discover their own patterns:

- "Tell me about a time when [competency situation] went well. What did you do?"
- "Tell me about a time when it didn't go well. What happened?"
- "What feedback have you received about this? From whom?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how comfortable are you with [competency]?"
- "If you could be significantly better at one thing in the next six months, what would have the biggest impact on your career?"
- "What does your manager/team/peers wish you did differently?"
- "What situations do you actively avoid? Why?"

### The Overuse Conversation

When you suspect overuse, approach with curiosity, not judgment:

- "You mentioned you're very action-oriented. That's a real strength. Have you ever received feedback that you move too fast or don't bring people along?"
- "Strong composure is valuable. Has anyone ever told you they wish they could read you better?"
- "You clearly drive results. Has that ever come at a cost you weren't comfortable with?"

### Framing Development Positively

Development is not about fixing deficits — it's about expanding range:

- "You don't need to stop being action-oriented. You need to add the ability to pause and plan when the situation calls for it."
- "This isn't about becoming a different person. It's about having more tools in your toolkit."
- "The goal is versatility — being able to flex between approaches depending on what the situation needs."

## Part IV: Career Stallers and Stoppers

Lombardo & Eichinger identified patterns that derail careers regardless of technical competence. Watch for these in the user's situation:

| Staller/Stopper | Description |
|---|---|
| **Blocked Personal Learner** | Doesn't learn from experience or feedback. Repeats mistakes. |
| **Lack of Composure** | Falls apart under pressure. Becomes defensive or emotional. |
| **Lack of Ethics/Integrity** | Cuts corners. Doesn't follow through. Breaks trust. |
| **Failure to Build a Team** | Can't assemble or lead a high-performing group. |
| **Failure to Staff** | Poor hiring decisions. Doesn't address poor performers. |
| **Key Skill Deficiencies** | Missing a critical technical or functional skill for the role. |
| **Non-Strategic** | Can't think beyond the immediate. Doesn't see the big picture. |
| **Overdependence on a Single Skill** | Relies on one strength (e.g., technical expertise) and neglects everything else. |
| **Overdependence on an Advocate** | Career depends on a single powerful sponsor. |
| **Political Missteps** | Doesn't read the organisation. Makes enemies unnecessarily. |
| **Too Narrow** | Hasn't had diverse experiences. Only knows one function/domain. |

If the user describes a career that has plateaued, systematically check for these stallers. They are often the real issue, even when the user thinks the problem is something else.
