# Occasional Modes: Elegy, Ode, Ekphrasis

## Overview

These three forms are not subjects but occasions — each creates a specific relationship between the poet and their material that shapes what's possible from the first line. They are not closed forms like the sonnet; they are open conventions with accumulated traditions the poet can use, resist, or transform.

---

## Elegy

### The Form's Demand

The elegy mourns. But mourning is not the elegy's purpose — it is its occasion. The elegy's purpose is to arrive somewhere the poet couldn't reach without the loss: a knowledge, a quality of attention, an understanding of the dead person that only their absence makes possible.

This is why elegies written too soon often fail. The elegy requires enough distance from the grief to see the loss clearly — to know what was lost specifically, not just that something is gone. The elegy that can only feel the loss cannot yet speak about it.

### The Three Movements (Classical and Contemporary)

**Lament** — the direct confrontation with loss. The speaker states the death and its impact. This is where the grief lives in its most elemental form. The lament must be specific — not "I have lost something precious" but the particular person, the particular thing that will not happen again, the specific absence the poet now inhabits.

**Praise** — the celebration of what was lost. The praise creates the presence the poem then has to release. It must be accurate, not hagiographic — the dead person as they actually were, including the contradictions, the difficult things, the ways the relationship was complicated. Hagiographic praise produces sentiment; accurate praise produces grief. The more specific and accurate the praise, the more the reader feels the loss.

**Consolation** — but not reassurance. The literary elegy does not offer comfort in the conventional sense; it does not promise reunion, heaven, or continued presence. What it offers is a different order of consolation: the knowledge that grief itself can become a form of attention to the lost person's reality. The elegist discovers, through writing the elegy, a way of holding the loss that is not resolution but presence — the dead person remains knowable, thinkable, speakable.

Auden's elegy for Yeats both performs the consolation and complicates it: "poetry makes nothing happen." The elegy acknowledges its own futility (the dead stays dead, poetry changes nothing) and proceeds anyway. The proceeding is the consolation.

### The Anti-Sentimentality Discipline

The elegy's greatest danger is that grief becomes the poet's subject rather than an act of attention to the dead. The sentimental elegy is about the elegist's feeling; the literary elegy is about the dead person. The difference shows in the direction of attention: the sentimental poem looks inward (how do I feel? how much have I lost?); the literary poem looks at the dead person with the intensity that only absence makes possible.

Tactics for avoiding sentiment:
- Stay close to the particular: the specific smell, gesture, word, object
- Include what was difficult about the relationship; the elegy that can only praise fails in accuracy
- Resist generalisation: the line that moves from the particular person to universal truths about death usually marks the moment the elegy gives up
- Allow the grief to include anger, ambivalence, the unresolved — the elegy does not require resolution

### Contemporary Elegy: Olds, Bidart, Sexton

Sharon Olds's *The Father* demonstrates what the elegy can achieve when it refuses to protect the reader from the complexity of the relationship. Her father was a source of damage as well as love; the elegy holds both without resolving the tension. The mourning is more complete because the relationship was more complete.

Frank Bidart's elegies (for his mother, for friends) work through the dramatic monologue tradition — the dead person is given a voice, which makes the elegy more disturbing than consoling. The dead speak, and what they say is not reassurance.

Anne Sexton's elegies register the grief in its most visceral form: the body fails, the mind fails, the self fails. Her elegies are anti-elegies in the sense that they refuse the consolation movement — grief is not resolved into meaning but held in its unresolved state. This refusal is itself a form of honesty.

## Ode

### The Form's Demand

The ode praises. But the literary ode is not flattery or gratitude expressed in verse. It is an act of radical attention that discovers the significance of its subject through the precision of the praise.

The ode addresses its subject directly — apostrophe. This is formally strange: addressing a nightingale, an urn, a west wind, as if they could hear, as if the address could produce an answer. The strangeness is the engine. The gulf between the speaker's intensity of address and the subject's muteness (the urn cannot answer; the wind does not know itself as beautiful) creates the poem's energy.

### The Keatsian Structure

Keats's great odes (*Nightingale*, *Grecian Urn*, *Psyche*, *Melancholy*, *Autumn*) demonstrate a consistent movement:

1. **The approach** — the speaker comes to the subject, encounters its beauty/power
2. **The aspiration** — the speaker wants to dissolve into the subject, to escape the human world of time and mortality into the subject's timelessness or freedom
3. **The recognition of limits** — the aspiration fails; the speaker cannot sustain the flight, cannot remain in the nightingale's world or inside the urn's timelessness
4. **The fall** — "forlorn! the very word is like a bell / To toll me back from thee to my sole self"
5. **What remains** — after the fall, what knowledge? The ode typically ends not with consolation but with a residue: the recognition of what cannot be had, which is also the recognition of what is actual

The structure is not pessimistic — it is realistic about the limits of aspiration. The ode earns its ending by failing at what it attempted.

### The Apostrophe Paradox

Direct address to something that cannot answer creates an unstable relationship between speaker and subject. The instability is productive:

- The urn "speaks" because Keats makes it speak; the ode is the act of projecting voice onto the mute object
- When the urn "speaks," it says something terrible: "Beauty is truth, truth beauty" — which is either profound consolation or meaningless circularity. The ode's ending refuses to resolve which
- The speaker knows the subject cannot answer. This knowledge is the ode's melancholy — the apostrophe is a form of loneliness, speaking to what cannot speak back

### Contemporary Ode: The Ode to the Ordinary

The most interesting contemporary development is the ode to ordinary, domestic, or uncomfortable subjects — Ross Gay's odes to gratitude, to sassafras, to the sweet potato; Sharon Olds's odes to the body. This expansion of the ode's traditional subjects (great natural forces, beautiful objects) creates a democratic poetics: anything worthy of real attention is worthy of an ode.

The radical claim is that radical attention is itself the praise — that the act of looking closely at a sweet potato, of genuinely seeing it, is an act of love and significance. This is not irony; it is the conviction that the ordinary is inexhaustible under real attention.

For the contemporary ode to succeed, the attention must be real. The poem cannot perform attention; it must actually look, actually find. The sweet potato must be stranger and more beautiful after the poem than before. If the poem simply names the potato as beautiful without doing the work of seeing, it fails.

## Ekphrasis

### The Form's Demand

Ekphrasis is poetry about visual art. At the literary level, it is not description — it is a conversation between two forms of attention, a negotiation between what the image shows and what the poem can do that the image cannot.

The ekphrastic poem must earn its existence. The image already exists; the poem must add something the image cannot provide. This is the ekphrastic challenge: what does poetry offer that the image withholds?

### The Three Modes

**Descriptive ekphrasis** — the poem describes the image. This is the least interesting mode: it produces what the reader can already see (or could see, with the image in front of them). Description is only the starting point.

**Interpretive ekphrasis** — the poem discovers a meaning in the image that is not visible to casual looking. The poem directs attention to what the image conceals — a shadow, a figure at the margin, an expression that could be read two ways, a historical context that changes what the image shows.

**Competitive/enactive ekphrasis** — the poem does what the image cannot do. It gives voice to the mute figures; it moves through time (the image is static; the poem can show what happened before and after); it reveals the emotion that the painted figure contains without expressing. Keats's "Ode on a Grecian Urn" is the paradigm: the poem gives the urn a voice ("Thou still unravish'd bride of quietness"), enters the frozen scene, and in doing so reveals the terror of the urn's stillness — "Cold Pastoral!" — that the urn itself cannot acknowledge. The urn is beautiful and terrible; the poem can say so; the urn cannot.

### Auden's Ekphrasis

Auden's "Musée des Beaux Arts" takes a different approach: it uses Brueghel's *Landscape with the Fall of Icarus* not to enter the painting but to reflect on what the painting shows about human indifference to suffering. The poem barely describes the painting; it meditates on what the painting depicts about the world. The ekphrasis is the occasion, not the subject.

This is also legitimate — the visual object as occasion for thinking that the poem could not have reached without it.

### What the Image Cannot Show

The most productive question for ekphrastic writing: where does this image fail? What can it not show?

- **Time** — the image is frozen at one moment; the poem can show the moment before and after
- **Interiority** — the painting shows a face; the poem can give the face a thought
- **Voice** — the figures are mute; the poem can make them speak
- **The reality outside the frame** — the painting shows only what is in the composition; the poem can show what the painter chose not to include
- **The passage of time since the image was made** — the poem can address the painting as an old thing, in our present time, and register the distance

### The Ekphrastic Error

The ekphrastic error is mistaking the image for reality — writing as if the painting were a window onto the world depicted, rather than an artefact with a maker, a history, and a relationship to the present viewer. The poem that treats the painting as if it were reality loses the productive friction between image and poem. The best ekphrasis is aware of the painting-as-object (old, made by someone, containing choices and exclusions) as well as the painting-as-image (appearing to depict a world).

## Using These Forms

### Elegy
- Identify what was specific about this person — the irreplaceable particular
- Identify the complexity of the relationship — what cannot be only praised
- Find the consolation that is not reassurance: what knowledge does the loss produce?
- The ending should not resolve grief but should hold it with the precision of full attention

### Ode
- Choose a subject because you genuinely want to know it better, not because it is conventionally praiseworthy
- Address it directly — use the second person; feel the strangeness of that address
- Let the aspiration fail: find where the flight cannot be sustained
- Discover what remains after the fall — this is the ode's actual ending

### Ekphrasis
- Stand in front of the image (or with an image in front of you) and look until you see something you didn't expect to see
- Ask: where does this image fail? What can it not show?
- Write toward the limit of the image — toward what the poem can do that the image cannot
- Do not describe what is already visible; add what is invisible
